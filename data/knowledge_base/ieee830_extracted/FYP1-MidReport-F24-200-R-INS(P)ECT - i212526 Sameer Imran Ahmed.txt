1.2 Research Problem Statement
Extensive research has been done on small object detection and specifically on insect
detection as well. However, these solutions require specialized hardware and can be ex-
pensive and difficult to implement for small-scale farmers. The automated solution that
our collaborator Dr. Abdul Bais’ team developed in [1010] is the Smart Insect Trap (SIT). It
is equipped with a camera which takes pictures of a sticky trap and uses a deep learning
model to determine the count of insects. This result is then uploaded to a cloud server
for analysis. However, this solutions is expensive and inaccessible requiring the use of
NVIDIA Jetson Nano GPU.
Our research focuses on providing a readily accessible and user-friendly solution to au-
tomate the process of pest detection. We will focus on the techniques to develop an AI
model capable of accurate pest detection with low energy consumption on edge devices.
We will then optimize this model for integration with edge devices so that it decreases the
inference time required while providing accurate counts for optimal planning of insecti-
cide use.

2.1.1 Research Item 1
A novel computer vision system for efficient flea beetle monitoring in canola crop by
Ullah et al. [1010]
2.1.1.1 Summary of the research item
This research paper presents a novel insect-monitoring system for canola crops that ad-
dresses the challenges of manual monitoring for flea beetles (FBs). The proposed so-
lution involves a solar-powered smart insect trap (SIT) equipped with a high-resolution
camera and a deep-learning-based detection network called FB-YOLO, which is a modi-
fied version of YOLOv8s. By enhancing the network’s neck to better aggregate features
from different layers, FB-YOLO improves the detection of small FBs, achieving an aver-
age precision (mAP@0.5) of 89.97%, which is a 1.215% improvement over the standard
YOLOv8s. Additionally, the integration of a confluence non-max suppression algorithm
(NMS-C) to reduce duplicate detections further boosts the mAP@0.5 to 90.16%. This
system enables hourly monitoring and rapid detection of FB population increases, en-
hancing crop health management.

2. Literature Review
2.1.1.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Some of the strengths of the paper include the introduction of a new object detection
model for the SITs,designed for flea beetle detection. The model is a modification of the
YOLOv8 architecture. The paper works on a comprehensive custom dataset collected
specifically for automating the processes of insect counting. The classification of images
based on FB density (sparse, medium, and dense) allows for targeted evaluation of model
performance. The paper also uses a special class-wise version of the non-max suppression
algorithm for better accuracy as opposed to the default one. Overall, the SIT is a neat
solution. However, there are some limitations as well. Firstly, the SITs are expensive
embedded systems containing hardware such as a high resolution camera and the Nvidia
Jetson Nano GPU. Apart from being expensive, these solutions are not easily accessible
to farmers. Secondly, there is a dependence on a cloud server for analysis, ruling out the
SITs from being true edge-devices. Lastly, the convolutional neural network (CNN) used
within the SITs struggles with integration on other platforms. Hence, there is a need for
research on developing an optimized beetle counting model for mobile phones.
2.1.1.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The paper serves as the primary basis for our research, providing valuable insights into
automated pest counting methods. We aim to address the gaps highlighted with our own
research, offering a more reliable solution to farmers. The current solution presented in
the paper is not only too expensive, but less readily available. Additionally, the reliance on
server-based processing further limits usability, especially in remote agricultural settings.
Our goal is to bring the solution to readily accessible mobile devices, allowing farmers to
utilize modern technology in their practices without having to go out of their way to buy
specialized equipment. For this purpose, we are collaborating with experts like Dr. Abdul
Bais and partnering with Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada, ensuring that we fill the gaps
identified in the paper and contribute to the advancement of agricultural practices.

by Zhong et al. [1212]
2.1.2.1 Summary of the research item
The paper presents a vision-based counting system designed to detect and classify flying
insects. The implementation of the system features the YOLO (You Only Look Once) [66]

ology involves using deep learning techniques for counting, and SVM for identification
between six insect species based on features such as color, texture, and shape. An average

system operates efficiently on a Raspberry PI, allowing for real-time monitoring of pest

2.1.2.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The paper highlights significant advancements in pest detection using deep learning. One
of the paper’s strengths lies in its ability to handle insufficient training data. In the first
phase, all flying insects are regarded as one class and are detected via deep learning. In the
second phase, SVM is used for fine classification. Another strength is the use of yellow
sticky traps in the wild for image collection, rather than in a controlled lab environment,
demonstrating the paper’s real-world application. The system runs on a Raspberry Pi
enabling edge-computing which is another notable strength. However, there are some
limitations as well. Firstly, the system’s reliance on a limited dataset raises questions
about generalizability. Secondly, while the system runs on edge, there is still a reliance
on a cloud server for analysis.
2.1.2.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The paper presents a vision-based system for detecting insects, which shares a common
goal with our proposed work of enhancing pest management through technological in-
novation. Both our work and the paper rely on yellow sticky traps for pest detection.
Our research specifically focuses on optimizing a deep learning model for mobile phones,
making pest counting more accessible and cost-effective for farmers. This is a different
approach compared to the paper which uses an embedded device and cloud-based anal-
ysis. Both our work and the paper utilize deep learning techniques, and we may draw
inspiration from their methodologies to inform and enhance our own approach.

2.1.3.1 Summary of the research item
The paper dives into the difficulties of detecting small objects within the field of com-
puter vision. A number of deep learning models were analyzed, namely YOLOv3[ 77],

2.1.5 Research Item 5
Faster R-CNN and EfficientNet[99] for accurate insect identification in a relabeled yellow
sticky traps dataset by Deserno et al. [11]
2.1.5.1 Summary of the research item
The paper discusses counting and classification of insects on yellow sticky traps using
deep learning models. The authors in the paper work on a public bench-marking dataset
which they first re-annotate due to mislabelling. The dataset contains three types of in-
sect classes. Two main methods are applied: Faster RCNN with inception ResNet-v2[ 33],
and EfficientNet. Data is augmented and split to improve model performance. Results
show Faster R-CNN achieved weighted average accuracies of 92.51% and 81.27%, while
EfficientNet-B3 reached an accuracy of 99.1% with high precision and recall rates. Effi-
cientNet also took significantly less time to train compared to Faster RCNN.
2.1.5.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The paper has a number of strengths worth discussing. The biggest strength is how the
authors provide extensive details regarding implementation, covering the entire process of
data preparation, data preprocessing, model specifics, hyper-parameters, training/testing
details etc. These details are followed by model comparisons on various metrics such
as accuracy, precision, recall, training time, and number of epochs. The authors also re-
annotate the dataset they use, keeping the newly labeled dataset public. Overall, the paper
is highly practical.
However a number of gaps have also been identified. Due to the paper’s highly practical
approach, a lack in theoretical explanations behind the results is felt. Furthermore, the
model comparisons in the paper do not include metrics such as inference times, nor do
they address resource consumption in training/testing. Hence, trade-offs between speed
and accuracy cannot be seen clearly. Lastly, the authors leave the problem of reducing
false positives to future research works without delving into any strategies to counter this
issue.
2.1.5.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The paper is highly relevant to our proposed work since it focuses on the same task of
counting and classifying different insect types on yellow sticky traps. The paper works
with three different species of insect while our work focuses on species of canola flea
beetle. The implementation details provided in the paper such as data pre-processing

Proposed Approach
This chapter outlines our proposed methodology to address the problems mentioned in
the first chapter. Our primary goal is to deploy a deep learning system on mobile phones
to count flea beetles on canola farms. Our approach experiments with several frame-
works, algorithms, and techniques discussed in Chapter 2 to ensure the requirements of

3.1 Research Methodology
We will work with the same dataset as identified in [ 1010] while addressing its limitations
related to expensive and relatively inaccessible specialized equipment. Our methodology
includes experimenting with various data preprocessing techniques such as augmentation
and image slicing to enhance model performance. We will choose our baseline models
from our literature review, particularly from [1212], [55] and [11] amongst others, and conduct
experiments to refine model selection through parameter adjustments and architectural
modifications. Additionally, we may need to explore alternative approaches, such as ma-
chine learning for classification as in the case of [ 1212] and two-stage detection methods
as in the case of [ 55]. Insights from the model implementation details in [ 11] will signif-
icantly aid us by providing us with practical details due to the similarity of our works.
Furthermore, [ 11] will also provide us with a public benchmarking dataset for compar-
isons. We will compare the different models on various metrics such as accuracy (mean
average precision), inference time, resource consumption, and energy consumption. After
selecting the optimal model, our primary focus will shift to optimization where we will
be utilizing techniques discussed in our reviewed literature, especially [ 1111], which deals
with mobile-specific optimizations. These optimizations aim to reduce model size and
inference time while addressing accuracy-energy trade-offs essential for mobile devices
which are constrained by battery. Extensive testing will be conducted, including eval-
