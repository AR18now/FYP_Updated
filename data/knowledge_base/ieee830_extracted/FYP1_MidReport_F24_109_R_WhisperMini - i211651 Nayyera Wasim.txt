1. Introduction
1.2 Research Problem Statement
The research focuses on improving Whisper’s efficiency in transcribing Urdu, particularly
for large-scale datasets. In our usecase we have approximately 6000 hours of call center
audio data generated daily. Currently, Whisper transcribes at a 1:1 ratio, meaning that
6000 hours of data would take 6000 hours to transcribe, which is computationally expen-
sive and impractical for real-time applications. Additionally, Whisper is not fine-tuned for
Urdu’s linguistic and phonetic characteristics, further reducing its transcription accuracy.
The core research problem is how to optimize Whisper to transcribe large datasets faster
and with greater accuracy for Urdu, while minimizing resource usage.
1.3 Software Requirements Specification (if applicable)
This project involves the development of a specialized version of Whisper, termed Whisper-
Mini, which incorporates several key optimizations:
• Non-Functional Requirements:
1. Efficient Transcription: The system should transcribe Urdu audio data more
quickly than the baseline Whisper model.
2. High Accuracy: The transcription accuracy for Urdu should be at least main-
tained or increased through fine-tuning with Urdu-specific datasets.
3. Web Interface: The system should provide a user-friendly web interface for
uploading and receiving transcribed audio.
1. Performance: The system should be able to transcribe large volumes of audio
data with reduced computational resources.
2. Scalability: The system should handle transcription requests for thousands of
hours of audio without degradation in performance.
3. Accuracy: The system should achieve a significant reduction in Word Error
Rate (WER) and Character Error Rate (CER) for Urdu.
These initial specifications provide the groundwork for optimizing Whisper to meet the
specific requirements of the target domain.

After the need for a robust ASR increased in the industry, [ 11]Radford et al proposed in-
creasing the robustness of ASR models through weakly supervised learning. This meant
that the data used was not up to golden standard and included noise. Thus, the model
was able to transcribe speech even in noisy scenarios. This would later end up becom-
ing the widely known Whisper by Open-AI. Many authors then furthered this research
by building on the concept of training a model on large weakly supervised datasets or
simply building up on Whisper. For example [ 22] Zhao et al uses the concept of zero-
shot learning, [33] Peng et al tries to mimmic and surpass Whisper’s performance through
e-branchformer and [ 44] Georgila et al used data augmentation to fine tune Whisper for
low resource languages. During our research review we came across multiple brilliant
approaches to achieving smaller WER for Speech to text systems, specifically for lower
language resources. However, there was a lack of focus on Urdu and a resource con-
strained environment. The following Research items outlines the research we reviewed
to identify the research gap and gain a better understanding of applicable models and
possible solutions to our use-case.

2.1.1 Robust Speech Recognition via Large-Scale Weak Supervision
Whisper AI
The paper [11] presents Whisper, a speech recognition system built on the concept of large-
scale weak supervision. Whisper is trained on 680,000 hours of multilingual and multi-
task supervised audio data from the internet. It focuses on zero-shot transfer learning
without fine-tuning. The approach improves on more conventional methods by elimi-
nating the need for adjustments in the dataset. It achieves near-human accuracy across
diverse environments. The paper compares Whisper’s performance to both traditional
supervised models and human transcription standards which highlights its robustness in
out-of-distribution settings. The system’s key points are: Multitask learning, multilingual
capabilities, and scaling large datasets.
2.1.1.1 Summary of the research item
This research focused on zero-shot transfer learning in the context of Automatic Speech
Recognition (ASR). Whisper models can generalize to new tasks and datasets without
explicit fine-tuning. They achieve this through training on large amounts of diverse, mul-
tilingual audio data. Key findings include Whisper’s competitive performance compared
to supervised systems and its robustness across various languages and acoustic environ-
ments. The method employs large-scale weak supervision, using open-source data from
the internet and creates a general-purpose ASR system that approaches human-level ro-
bustness and accuracy. Whisper also used techniques such as text normalization and zero-
shot evaluation, focusing on generalization beyond conventional benchmark datasets.

2.1.2 Automatic speech recognition (ASR) with Whisper: Testing Per-
formances in Different Languages.
This research [ 22] examines how well OpenAI’s Whisper ASR model performs for both
healthy participants and those who are depressed in three different languages: English,
Italian, and Russian. The amount of correct, deleted, substituted, and inserted words as
well as the Word Error Rates (WER) for participants reading a standard fable in each of
these languages are among the important metrics used in the study to assess Whisper’s
transcription accuracy.

2.1.2.1 Summary of the research item
In this study, audio recordings from 226 participants—who were categorized by language
and mental health status (healthy vs. depressed)—were transcribed using Whisper. The
fable "The Northern Wind and the Sun" by Aesop was assigned to the participants to read
in their native tongues. Standard criteria, including accurate words, deletion, replacement,
insertion, and WER, were used to assess Whisper’s transcribing performance. Overall, the
results showed good transcription accuracy, with just minor differences between people
who were sad and those who were in good condition. Russian had a greater insertion
rate, while English and Italian displayed larger substitution errors. All languages had
consistently low WER.
2.1.2.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Strengths:
• Cross-linguistic application: Whisper’s performance in three different languages
shows its robustness in multilingual ASR systems.
• Comprehensive dataset: The use of both healthy and depressed participants pro-
vides valuable insights into how mental health can influence speech transcription
accuracy.
• Accuracy metrics: The detailed analysis of transcription errors (correct, deleted,
substituted, and inserted words) alongside WER makes the evaluation thorough.
Weaknesses:
• Clinical group bias : The study found that Whisper performed better for healthy
participants in English and Italian, but there was less difference in Russian. The
potential bias in transcription for clinical groups requires further exploration.
• Language variations: While Whisper handles multiple languages, the higher sub-
stitution and insertion rates in certain languages, such as Russian, suggest room for
improvement in its handling of less commonly spoken languages.
2.1.2.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
This study is quite relevant to our suggested work on resource-constrained Whisper for
Urdu optimization. Whisper’s multilingual capabilities can direct the creation of a strong
ASR for Urdu. The techniques employed here for error analysis, such as tracking par-
ticular word-level errors and analyzing WER, can be modified to evaluate and enhance
Whisper’s performance on Urdu language tasks.

2.1.3 Code-Switched Urdu ASR for noisy Telephonic Environment
Using Data Centric approach with Hybrid HMM and CNN-
TDNN
This research [ 55] discusses the performance of a proposed Automatic Speech Recog-
nition (ASR) system for code-switched Urdu in noisy telephonic environments. Hid-
den Markov Models (HMM) and Deep Neural Networks (DNN) are used to create the
ASR system. Specifically, a Convolutional Neural Network-Time Delay Neural Network
(CNN-TDNN) with a Lattice Free Maximum Mutual Information (LF-MMI) objective
function is used. With limited resources, the system strives for a low Word Error Rate
(WER) and Sentence Error Rate (SER) while managing the intricacies of code-switched
Roman Urdu.
2.1.3.1 Summary of the research item
The proposed ASR system tries to address the challenges of building a robust code-
switched ASR model for telephonic environments. The researchers created a data-centric
approach, collecting code-switched Urdu telephonic data and training the ASR model on
it. The aim is to develop a system that can handle code-switched Roman Urdu with high
accuracy in a call center setting. But also utilize minimal resources in terms of time, com-
puting power, and memory. The model satisfies the aim of a WER below 10% and SER
below 30%, and it can achieve an accuracy of 95% with only 10 hours of labeled data.
2.1.3.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Strengths:
• Data-Centric Approach: The focus on data collection and quality labeling ensures
the system is built on strong foundations, emphasizing the importance of data over
model tweaking.
• Resource Efficiency: The system is designed to be deployed in environments with
limited computational resources, making it practical for call center applications in
developing regions.
• Performance: The use of CNN-TDNN and LF-MMI for acoustic modeling enables
the system to handle noisy, telephonic audio while achieving strong WER and SER
performance.
Weaknesses:

the system’s performance on larger-scale applications or more diverse telephonic

• Code-Switching Complexity: While the system is optimized for Roman Urdu, the

2.1.4 Better and Faster Open Whisper-Style Speech Models based on
E-Branchformer

Whisper-Style Speech Models. The authors aimed to reproduce and enhance the per-
formance of OpenAI’s Whisper model using publicly available data and an open-source
toolkit. The key innovation in OWSM v3.1 is the introduction of E-Branchformer en-
coders, which outperform previous Transformer-based models used in prior OWSM ver-
sions. This research highlights how OWSM v3.1 achieves state-of-the-art performance
across various speech processing tasks while also improving inference speeds by up to
25%. The study emphasizes the scalability and efficiency of OWSM v3.1 in large-scale
speech recognition and translation tasks.

The OWSM v3.1 model series is an extension of previous Whisper-based models, focused
on improving performance using E-Branchformer. This captures local and global infor-
mation more effectively than standard encoders. The researchers tested their models on
benchmarks for Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) and Speech Translation (ST) tasks
and found significant improvements in both performance and efficiency. They report bet-
ter results on English and multilingual ASR and Speech Translation (X-to-English and

2.1.6 Improvement in Automatic Speech Recognition of South Asian
Accent Using Transfer Learning of DeepSpeech2
The research item [77] discusses the improvements in automatic speech recognition (ASR)
for non-native English accents, specifically focusing on South Asian accents. The study
builds upon the existing DeepSpeech2 model and explores how transfer learning can en-
hance the accuracy of ASR systems for non-native accents. The research aims to address
the challenges faced by ASR systems, which are often trained on datasets featuring native
English accents, leading to reduced accuracy for non-native speakers.
2.1.6.1 Summary of the research item
By adjusting the DeepSpeech2 model, the study offers a way to enhance ASR perfor-
mance for South Asian dialects. Using a portion of the Common V oice dataset that ex-
clusively contains South Asian accents, the researchers retrained the model. In order to
maximize feature learning for these accents, three alternative layer topologies are evalu-
ated. As evaluation criteria, the study uses Word Error Rate (WER), Match Error Rate
(MER), and Word Information Lost (WIL). WER for non-native accents was significantly
reduced in the final model, falling from 43% to 18.08%.

• Transfer learning approach: The use of transfer learning to adapt a pre-trained

2.1.7 Learning Cross-lingual Mappings for Data Augmentation to
Improve Low-Resource Speech Recognition
This research [88] studies an end-to-end cross-lingual voice recognition system that uses
data augmentation and mapping models to handle low-resource languages is covered in

this study. The system is primarily concerned with translating voice data between lan-
guages. It is especially helpful for low-resource languages such as Tamil, Telugu, Ce-
buano, and Javanese. The essential contribution comes in generating ciphered transcrip-
tions using source language ASR systems and applying mapping models to complement
the destination language ASR systems.
2.1.7.1 Summary of the research item
For the purpose of mapping posterior distributions from source language ASR models to
target language ASR models, the paper presents a multi-encoder, single-decoder (MESD)
architecture. By translating source language posteriors into the target language, cross-
lingual speech recognition is made possible without the need for parallel data. Thirty
hours were chosen for training from each language in the BABEL dataset. The method
is focused on data augmentation. The audio from the source language is transliterated
into the target language to produce ciphered text used to retrain the target ASR system.
Character error rate (CER) improvements of up to 28.5% as compared to monolingual
systems are shown by the results.
2.1.7.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Strengths:
• Cross-lingual capability: The ability to map between languages without parallel
data offers a significant breakthrough for low-resource speech recognition tasks.
• Data augmentation : The integration of ciphered text as an augmentation tech-
nique provides a novel way to increase training data for under-resourced languages,
demonstrating notable improvements in CER.
Weaknesses:
• Mapping accuracy: The accuracy of the mapping models varies between language
pairs, with some languages (e.g., Tamil and Telugu) performing worse than oth-
ers (e.g., Cebuano and Javanese), likely due to the differences in character set and
linguistic structure.
• Transliteration limitations: The system’s performance deteriorates for longer ut-
terances, suggesting that the method may struggle with more complex speech pat-
terns in low-resource languages.

3.2.3 QLoRA (Quantized Low-Rank Adaptation)
: To inmprove the efficiency of model training and inference, we will implement QLoRA,
which allows for efficient fine-tuning of large language models with significantly reduced
memory requirements. QLoRA first quantizes the model, reducing the numerical preci-
sion of model parameters. This minimizes the memory needed to run these models. For
example, instead of using 16 bits to store a parameter, we can represent it using 8 bits,
which is essential for real-time performance.
3.2.3.1 Combining Techniques
With QLoRA, we can fine-tune the model without sacrificing precision by training smaller
low-rank matrices that approximate the original parameters. This process speeds up the
fine-tuning and also makes it feasible for those with limited computational resources.
For example, while a full fine-tuning of a 70 billion parameter model typically requires
780GB of GPU memory, QLoRA allows us to achieve this with only 48GB, allowing
easier access to advanced model fine-tuning.

2.1.1 Robust Speech Recognition via Large-Scale Weak Supervision
Whisper AI
The paper [11] presents Whisper, a speech recognition system built on the concept of large-
scale weak supervision. Whisper is trained on 680,000 hours of multilingual and multi-
task supervised audio data from the internet. It focuses on zero-shot transfer learning
without fine-tuning. The approach improves on more conventional methods by elimi-
nating the need for adjustments in the dataset. It achieves near-human accuracy across
diverse environments. The paper compares Whisper’s performance to both traditional
supervised models and human transcription standards which highlights its robustness in
out-of-distribution settings. The system’s key points are: Multitask learning, multilingual
capabilities, and scaling large datasets.
2.1.1.1 Summary of the research item
This research focused on zero-shot transfer learning in the context of Automatic Speech
Recognition (ASR). Whisper models can generalize to new tasks and datasets without
explicit fine-tuning. They achieve this through training on large amounts of diverse, mul-
tilingual audio data. Key findings include Whisper’s competitive performance compared
to supervised systems and its robustness across various languages and acoustic environ-
ments. The method employs large-scale weak supervision, using open-source data from
the internet and creates a general-purpose ASR system that approaches human-level ro-
bustness and accuracy. Whisper also used techniques such as text normalization and zero-
shot evaluation, focusing on generalization beyond conventional benchmark datasets.

it. The aim is to develop a system that can handle code-switched Roman Urdu with high
accuracy in a call center setting. But also utilize minimal resources in terms of time, com-
puting power, and memory. The model satisfies the aim of a WER below 10% and SER
below 30%, and it can achieve an accuracy of 95% with only 10 hours of labeled data.
