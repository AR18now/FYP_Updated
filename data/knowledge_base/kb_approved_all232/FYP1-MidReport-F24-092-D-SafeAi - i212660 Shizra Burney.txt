1.3 Software Requirements Specification
The SAFEAi project involves the development of an AI-based software system for real-
time object detection, tracking, and re-identification from high-altitude UA V footage. The
system will leverage deep learning models and advanced computer vision techniques.
The following outlines the functional and non-functional requirements necessary for its
successful implementation:
1.3.1 Functional Requirements:
• Object Detection: The system must detect multiple objects (vehicles, people, etc.)
in aerial images using YOLOv8 and Faster R-CNN algorithms, providing accurate
bounding boxes around objects of interest.
• Small Object Detection : Implement the SAHI (Slicing Aided Hyper Inference)
technique to handle large images by dividing them into patches, improving the de-
tection accuracy of small objects.
• Object Tracking: Use DeepSORT for tracking multiple objects over time, main-
taining object identity and tracking through frames.
• Re-identification: The system must re-identify objects that leave and reappear in
the field of view, using CNN-based feature extraction for reliable identification
across different frames and cameras.
• Real-time Processing: The system must provide real-time detection and tracking
with minimal latency to ensure its applicability in dynamic surveillance environ-
ments.

2.1 Related Research
2.1.1 Research Item 1: The Unmanned Aerial Vehicle Benchmark:
Object Detection and Tracking (Dawei Du et al., 2018) [11]
2.1.1.1 Summary of the research item
This research presents the UA VDT dataset, a comprehensive Unmanned Aerial Vehicle
(UA V) benchmark designed to advance object detection, single-object tracking (SOT),
and multi-object tracking (MOT) in complex, real-world scenarios. The dataset com-
prises 100 video sequences, containing approximately 80,000 annotated frames selected
from over 10 hours of footage captured from urban environments. The dataset is unique
in its focus on unconstrained settings, which introduces challenging conditions such as
varying weather, flying altitudes, camera views, and high-density traffic scenarios. A
wide range of attributes—including vehicle categories, occlusions, and out-of-view con-
ditions—are annotated to provide a detailed dataset for testing state-of-the-art computer
vision algorithms. Through extensive experimentation with recent object detection and
tracking algorithms, the study identifies gaps in current methods, particularly highlight-
ing difficulties related to small object detection, real-time performance, and the challenges
posed by camera motion.

2. Literature Review
2.1.1.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The UA VDT benchmark represents a significant contribution to the field of UA V-based
vision systems by providing a well-annotated dataset for three fundamental tasks. The
authors’ efforts in annotating 80,000 frames with over 840,000 bounding boxes demon-
strate the rigor and scale necessary for benchmarking. One of the strengths of the study
is its focus on real-world, unconstrained scenarios, which presents more practical chal-
lenges compared to controlled datasets like those focusing on parking lots or fixed-angle
cameras. The UA VDT dataset fills a gap in UA V datasets by introducing complex urban
environments with multiple attributes, such as occlusions, varying altitudes, and lighting
conditions, which are essential for developing robust algorithms. However, a limitation
of this research is the performance of state-of-the-art algorithms on this dataset, as they
struggle with small objects and high-density tracking, which suggests that existing models
may need significant enhancements to handle UA V-specific challenges. Another limita-
tion lies in the computational cost, as many accurate algorithms such as Faster R-CNN and
MDP perform below real-time, making them unsuitable for UA V platforms with limited
resources.
2.1.1.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The insights gained from the UA VDT dataset are directly relevant to our proposed re-
search, which aims to improve real-time multi-object tracking for UA V-based surveil-
lance. The challenges identified in the UA VDT benchmark, particularly in tracking small
objects and handling occlusions, align with the objectives of this study, as we aim to
develop more efficient algorithms that can process complex visual data in real time. Ad-
ditionally, the benchmark’s inclusion of multiple object tracking and real-time constraints
offers a useful testing ground for the evaluation of our proposed methodologies. The
detailed analysis of the UA VDT benchmark’s shortcomings also provides a clear direc-
tion for addressing gaps in current models, such as improving detection in high-density
scenarios and reducing computational overhead.

2.1.3 Research Item 3: Object Tracking and Counting in a Zone us-
ing YOLOv4, DeepSORT, and TensorFlow (Kumar et al., 2021)
[33]
2.1.3.1 Summary of the research item
This paper presents a system for tracking and counting objects within a specified zone us-
ing YOLOv4 for object detection and DeepSORT for tracking. The model performs multi-
object detection and tracking by first identifying objects in the frame and then continu-
ously tracking them over multiple frames. Kalman filters are applied to improve motion
prediction, while DeepSORT extends the Simple Online and Realtime Tracking (SORT)
algorithm by incorporating a deep association metric to reduce identity switching during
tracking. The proposed system also introduces zonal counting, where objects are moni-
tored within a predefined area, raising alarms when the count exceeds a threshold. This
system has wide-ranging applications, such as traffic monitoring, managing COVID-19
restrictions, and crowd control, making it highly versatile for real-world scenarios.
2.1.3.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Kumar et al.’s work on object tracking and counting leverages state-of-the-art models
to offer real-time solutions for zone-specific monitoring. The integration of YOLOv4
with DeepSORT provides a powerful combination for accurate detection and tracking,
and the use of Kalman filters enhances robustness against occlusion, a major challenge
in crowded environments. The performance of the system on the MOT17 dataset shows
strong results, with the system achieving competitive MOTA (Multiple Object Tracking
Accuracy) scores. One limitation of the research is the computational intensity required
by the DeepSORT model, particularly when dealing with high-density object tracking,
which may impede scalability in UA V applications. Additionally, while YOLOv4 im-
proves detection speed and accuracy, the paper acknowledges challenges in handling ad-
verse weather conditions or low-light environments, which are critical for UA V-based
surveillance.
2.1.3.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The methodologies in this research are highly relevant to the goals of the SAFEAi project,
particularly in enhancing real-time object tracking from aerial surveillance. The zonal
counting feature, along with DeepSORT’s capability for persistent tracking, aligns with
SAFEAi’s need to monitor and track multiple small objects in complex environments,
such as disaster zones or border areas. The real-time nature of the system ensures that

2.1.4 Research Item 4:Clustered Object Detection in Aerial Images
(ICCV 2019)[55]
2.1.4.1 Summary of the research item
This paper presents the Clustered Detection (ClusDet) network, a novel approach to ob-
ject detection in aerial images. The main challenge in aerial imagery is the small size
and sparse distribution of objects. ClusDet addresses these challenges by using a Clus-
ter Proposal Network (CPNet) to identify object clusters, a Scale Estimation Network
(ScaleNet) to normalize object scales, and a Detection Network (DetecNet) to perform
detection within these clusters. ClusDet improves detection accuracy and efficiency by
focusing on clustered regions, reducing the number of regions that require detailed anal-
ysis. The system achieves state-of-the-art results on datasets like VisDrone, UA VDT, and
DOTA.
2.1.4.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The Clustered Object Detection in Aerial Images paper introduces an efficient method for
detecting objects in clustered regions, which reduces computational costs and improves
accuracy for small objects by utilizing the Cluster Proposal Network (CPNet) and Scale
Estimation Network (ScaleNet). While this approach excels in scenarios with object clus-
tering, its reliance on clustering limits its performance in non-clustered environments.
Additionally, the integration of multiple sub-networks, such as CPNet and DetecNet,
increases computational complexity, which could hinder real-time processing. Despite
these limitations, the method demonstrates a significant advancement in small-object de-
tection, although it might require further optimization for real-time applications.
2.1.4.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
Our project, SAFEAI, aims to enhance high-altitude aerial surveillance by improving ob-
ject detection, tracking, and re-identification. Like ClusDet, our approach also tackles the
challenge of small object detection. However, SAFEAI adds a new dimension by inte-
grating tracking and re-identification across frames. Furthermore, our proposal leverages

2.1.5 Research Item 5:An Experimental Study of Vehicle Detection
on Aerial Imagery Using Deep Learning-based Detection Ap-
proaches (2020) [44]
2.1.5.1 Summary of the research item
This paper compares several state-of-the-art deep learning models, including Faster R-
CNN, R-FCN, and SSD, for vehicle detection in aerial imagery. The experiments are
conducted on the VEDAI dataset, with Faster R-CNN (ResNet101 backbone) achieving
the highest mean Average Precision (mAP) score of 39.73 percent. The paper highlights
the performance trade-offs between detection accuracy and computational efficiency, not-
ing that two-stage detectors like Faster R-CNN tend to perform better than one-stage
detectors like SSD in aerial imagery tasks, particularly when handling small objects like
vehicles.
2.1.5.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The paper provides a valuable comparison of deep learning models for vehicle detection
in aerial imagery, emphasizing the strengths of two-stage detectors like Faster R-CNN
for handling small objects. Its practical analysis helps guide model selection for similar
tasks, but its focus on vehicle detection limits the broader application to other object types
or more complex aerial surveillance. SSD’s poor performance in small object detection
highlights its limitations in low-resolution data. Despite this, the paper offers important
insights that can inspire further optimization.
2.1.5.3 Relationship to the proposed research work

altitude surveillance, incorporating methods like SAHI to enhance small-object detection
efficiency. Moreover, our approach extends beyond detection by addressing real-time
tracking and re-identification, which is critical in security and surveillance operations.

3.0.0.1 3.1 Overview
The SAFEAi project aims to enhance aerial surveillance by developing an advanced AI-
driven system that provides accurate object detection, tracking, and re-identification in
real-time. The system addresses the key challenges posed by high-altitude surveillance,
such as the difficulty in detecting small objects, variations in appearance, and environ-
mental conditions like lighting and occlusion. The solution leverages state-of-the-art deep
learning models and computer vision techniques to process UA V (Unmanned Aerial Ve-
hicle) data efficiently and robustly.
The proposed system incorporates various AI and computer vision methodologies, in-
cluding object detection algorithms, tracking models, and feature extraction techniques,
to ensure reliable detection and tracking across multiple scenarios.
3.0.0.2 3.2 Proposed Solution Workflow
3.2.1 Step 1: High-Altitude Image Processing and Preprocessing The first step in
our solution is capturing high-altitude aerial images and video streams from UA Vs. These
images are large-scale and often contain a high level of complexity. To address this, we
will apply the following methods:
• Slicing Aided Hyper Inference (SAHI): Large-scale images are divided into smaller
patches for more accurate small object detection. This method allows the system to
focus on finer details in each patch, increasing detection accuracy for small objects
that are difficult to identify from a distance.
• Image Preprocessing: The system will preprocess images by normalizing lighting
conditions, removing noise, and enhancing contrast to make objects more visible
for detection algorithms.

3.2.2 Step 2: Object Detection Once the images are preprocessed, the next step is to
detect objects within the UA V-captured footage. We will utilize deep learning models
designed for real-time object detection:
• YOLOv8 (You Only Look Once version 8) : This is a fast and accurate object
detection model optimized for small objects. YOLOv8 will be used to detect objects
in each frame and provide bounding boxes around the detected objects.
• Faster R-CNN (Region-based Convolutional Neural Network) : For situations
where high precision is needed, such as detecting objects in crowded or occluded
environments, Faster R-CNN will be employed. This model excels at handling
complex object structures and multi-class detection.

3.2.3 Step 3: Object Tracking After detecting objects in the UA V footage, the system
needs to track their movement across multiple frames to ensure continuous monitoring.
We will implement the following tracking algorithms:
• DeepSORT (Simple Online and Realtime Tracking with a Deep Association
Metric): DeepSORT will be used for real-time object tracking by assigning a
unique ID to each detected object and maintaining the association of the object
across frames. DeepSORT’s appearance descriptor reduces identity switching, a
critical feature when tracking multiple small objects in high-density areas.
• Kalman Filters: Kalman Filters will assist in motion prediction, helping to main-
tain the tracking of objects even when they are temporarily occluded or move out
of the camera’s field of view.
Figure 3.3: Object Tracking with Deepsort
3.2.4 Step 4: Object Re-identification Re-identification is an essential feature of the
SAFEAi system, particularly for long-duration surveillance where objects may leave and
re-enter the camera’s view or appear across different UA Vs. The following methods will
be used for robust re-identification:
• Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs): CNNs will be used for extracting dis-
tinctive features from objects (such as color, texture, and shape) to enable re-identification.
Even when objects are viewed from different angles or under different lighting con-
ditions, the CNN-based feature extractor will match them across frames.

• Metric Learning: A distance metric will be used to compare objects across frames
and re-identify them based on their appearance features. The system will learn
to recognize similarities between objects using deep metric learning techniques,
minimizing identity mismatches.
3.2.5 Step 5: Real-time Processing and System Optimization Given the high de-
mands for real-time surveillance, optimizing the system for speed and efficiency is crucial.
The following strategies will be employed to achieve real-time processing:
• Hardware Optimization: The system will run on edge devices and UA V hard-
ware equipped with GPUs, ensuring low-latency processing. Lighter versions of
the models, such as YOLOv4-tiny, will be considered for UA Vs with limited com-
putational capacity.
• Parallel Processing: We will implement parallel processing on GPU cores to han-
dle multiple image streams simultaneously, allowing for real-time surveillance across
multiple UA Vs.
• Frame Skipping: To balance the load, the system will dynamically adjust frame
rates by skipping non-essential frames during low-activity periods, ensuring re-
sources are focused on high-activity scenarios.
3.0.0.3 3.3 Challenges and Solutions
3.3.1 Small Object Detection Small object detection is a significant challenge in aerial
surveillance due to high altitudes and the resolution limits of cameras. Our solution:
• Employs SAHI to process image patches separately, which increases detection ac-
curacy for small objects.
• Utilizes YOLOv8, which is optimized for small-object detection in complex envi-
ronments.
3.3.2 Real-Time Tracking Under Occlusions Objects may be occluded by buildings,
trees, or other objects in the environment, making it difficult to track them consistently.
To mitigate this:
• DeepSORT ensures identity consistency across frames, even with occlusions.
• Kalman Filters predict object movements during periods of occlusion, helping
maintain accurate tracking.
