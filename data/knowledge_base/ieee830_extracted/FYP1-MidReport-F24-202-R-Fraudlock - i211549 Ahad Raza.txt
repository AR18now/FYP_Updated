2.1 Related Research
2.1.1 FAMM: Facial Muscle Motions for Detecting Compressed Deep-
fake Videos Over Social Networks
2.1.1.1 Summary of the Research Item
Paper "FAMM: Facial Muscle Motions for Detecting Compressed Deepfake Videos Over So-
cial Networks"[55] addresses the menace of Deepfake videos, particularly those disseminated
over social networks wherein video compression is inescapable. The authors have advocated
a framework called FAMM (Facial-Muscle-Motions-Based), which improves the detection of
compressed Deepfake videos. The methodology consists of three modules: facial landmark
extraction, facial muscle motion feature construction, and probability prediction fusion.
Firstly, for a particular video, the pipeline picks facial landmarks from consecutive frames
through suitable face alignment techniques and DLIB. These facial landmarks then form geo-
metric features which capture the muscle movement in five different sensory areas along with
the entire face. The temporal dynamics of these movements then create patterns for detec-
tion as unnatural, thus suggesting Deepfakes by FAMM. A final detection is developed based
on Dempster-Shafer theory by fusing the probability scores from both GRU and SVM classi-
fiers to ensure more robustness to compression artifacts. Extensive experiments demonstrate
the superiority of FAMM in compressed Deepfake video detections, holding even accuracy in
released Facebook and YouTube videos.

2. Literature Review
2.1.1.2 Critical Analysis of the Research Item
The FAMM framework has several advantages that make the above solution effective for Deep-
fake detection. First, the application of facial muscle motion features is quite unique for giving
a geometric view beyond the conventional artifact-based detection methods. It is based on the
continuity of natural facial movements, which works to fight such noise commonly seen with
compression in videos found on social networks. Deep feature construction, uses both the dis-
tance and angle metrics over different facial regions, increasing the model’s ability to counter
subtle manipulations.
Further, Dempster-Shafer theory for probability fusion gives a good integration of the strengths
of GRU and SVM classifiers to improve the overall detection accuracy. Further, the theoretical
analysis on mutual information and invariance of facial landmarks even after compression adds
on to the creditworthiness of the robustness of the framework. However, the work also has
some drawbacks. The method based on precise facial landmark extraction sometimes faces
the problem of low-resolution or heavily occluded videos that can affect the feature accuracy.
More than that, The superior performance on standard datasets demonstrates the work well;
however, its adaptability to diverse and evolving Deepfake generation techniques has not been
adequately evaluated in this work. The computational complexity of processing continuous
facial landmarks and temporal features can further affect the scalability of this model, especially
for real-time applications on large-scale social networks.
Overall, FAMM holds promising potential in the area of Deepfake detection, especially with
regards to compressed videos. Future work can improve the robustness of the framework for
landmark extraction, and develop its adaptability to more techniques for making the model
rock-solid.
2.1.1.3 Relationship to the Proposed Research Work
Our proposed method is the extension of FAMM framework, taking visual and audio data to
further improve Deepfake detection. Focusing only on facial muscle movements in compressed
videos, our method also includes lip-audio synchronization analysis for cheek muscles. We can
better detect mismatches in lip movements with the audio by aligning features of audio with
frames of the video. Our use of attention mechanisms supports better focusing of the model on
key areas, which improves the accuracy of detection.
Unlike the approach adopted in the FAMM, where the Dempster-Shafer theory combines GRU
and SVM classifiers, the present proposal adopts MLP with feature fusion techniques in order
to combine the predictions from different modules in a way of simplifying the architecture
while offering an effective combination of different feature sets.

2.1.2 Preventing DeepFake Attacks on Speaker Authentication by Dy-
namic Lip Movement Analysis
2.1.2.1 Summary of the research item
This paper talks about the vulnerability of lip-based speaker authentication systems to Deep-
Fake attacks by focusing on the analysis of dynamic lip movements rather than by relying on
static features that are vulnerable to manipulation. In their work titled "Preventing DeepFake
Attacks on Speaker Authentication by Dynamic Lip Movement Analysis," [66] the authors pro-
pose a visual authentication scheme using a Deep Convolutional Neural Network (DCNN). A
proposed model called SA-DTH-Net is divided into two major parts: the Fundamental Feature
Extraction Network (FFE-Net) for dynamic lip motion analysis and the Representative Lip Fea-
ture Extraction and Classification Network (RC-Net) for the purpose of extracting high-level
features. It focuses more on speaker authentication via liveness through dynamic lip move-
ments, thus reducing the threat from static lip features vulnerable to DeepFake technology.
The model clearly demonstrated excellent performance on the GRID and MOBIO datasets,
thereby outperforming the authentication accuracy of three state-of-the-art visual speaker au-
thentication algorithms against human imposters. Another strength of the work was on its
robustness against DeepFake-generated attacks and ensured that the usage of multitask learn-
ing within the RC-Net contributes to reliable DeepFake detection by optimizing the network
for both feature reconstruction and classification accuracy. Importantly, this proposed method
does not rely on knowledge of specific DeepFake spoofing techniques, thus is flexible with
differing spoofing strategies.
2.1.2.2 Critical Analysis of the Research Item
In a principal strength, the paper introduces a novel architecture in SA-DTH-Net which in-
tegrates dynamic lip movement analysis along with the implementation of robust feature ex-
traction mechanisms. By prioritizing temporal dynamics over static features, the described
method complicates liveness detection and makes the authentication process even more dif-
ficult to deceive with DeepFake technologies. The addition of the multitask learning within
RC-Net further strengthens the model because of the high discriminative ability of the feature
extracted for authentication purposes, but also representative of individual talking styles, which
enhances the system’s resilience against impersonation attacks.
However, the study has certain limitations. Mostly, reliance on prerecorded data may impede
the model’s performance when applied to increasingly diversified and variable datasets that
are often faced during real-world scenarios. There could also exist variability in real-world
environment settings-lighting, camera angles, and occlusions introduced to the system that
may impact the accuracy of authentication. The computational cost of this presented network,
mainly its training, has the potential to affect the deploymentability of the system to resource-

2.1.3.2 Critical analysis of the research item
One of the strong points of this approach is the ability to represent features efficiently. In this
research, the investigator uses only facial landmarks, rather than whole images which is a very
efficient way of deepfake detection. The method is also simplified as it concentrates only on the
geometry of the face eliminating unnecessary complexities associated with analyzing images
on a pixel level. In addition, a Landmark Calibration module using the Lucas-Kanade optical
flow method and Kalman filter is added to deal with the problem of very jittery landmarks
that lead to noise and inaccurate results. The LK algorithm tracks movement and the Kalman
filter reduces the noise introduced by jittery landmarks. Another advantage is that it employs
a Two Stream RNN in which one stream accommodates the face shape while the other stream
takes the image frames over a period of time. This decision improves the system’s ability to
discriminate between inconsistencies in the structure of the face and its motion, which is very
important for deepfake detection. In addition, the method is quite flexible and comes with wide
applicability as it does not rely on any particular technique of landmarking which enhances its
generalization and adaptability to different datasets.
Nonetheless, this methodology has certain limitations. Due to the forward-backward check of
the previous and next frames the use of the Kalman filter may improve the accuracy and reduce
the noise but it introduces additional complexity and computational overhead. Continuous
forward and backward checks, slow down the real-time detection. The selection of parameters
like inherent variance Q and approximate relative variance Dr is empirical. It may work well
for controlled experiments but may not generalize to real-world situations where factors like
lighting could lead to inaccurate results and variances are difficult to predict or highly variable.

2.1.4 DF-TransFusion: Multimodal Deepfake Detection via Lip-Audio
Cross-Attention and Facial Self-Attention
2.1.4.1 Summary of the research item
In the paper "DF-TransFusion: Multimodal Deepfake Detection via Lip-Audio Cross-Attention
and Facial Self-Attention"[88], the authors propose an innovative deepfake detection system that
utilizes deep learning techniques to analyze both audio and video data.The architecture employs
a modified VGG-16 feature extractor for video data while audio data and lip regions are pro-
cessed in parallel using an audio transformer encoder. Face and lip regions of the videos are
very accurately obtained with the help of MTCNN which acts as an adjunct for the region of
interest extraction process. They used tubelet embedding method which captures spatial and
temporal relationships between frames at the same time, unlike the uniform frame sampling
approach, where each 2D frame is tokenized separately and temporal embedding information
needs to be provided independently to the transformer encoder. This embedding strategy en-
ables to promotion of the efficiency of the model by making it much easier to perform cross-
attention between lip movement and audio by making it easier for the model to tell whether the
audio and the lips are properly synchronized or not which is one of the features that deepfakes
tend to alter. Finally, the outputs from both the audio and video transformer encoder mod-
ules were then passed through the head of a multilayer perceptron (MLP) to generate the final
classification label.
The study concludes that this multimodal approach outperforms models that analyze modalities
independently. They highlight the potential of multimodal analysis in deepfake detection. It
emphasizes the need for continuous adoption of these models to newer deepfake generation
methods.

2.1.4.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The strengths of this research are notable. First, the use of VGG-16 for feature extraction and
a transformer encoder with self-attention and cross-attention mechanisms, which are highly
effective for handling sequential data such as video and audio. This combination seems well-
chosen, as VGG-16 is known for its efficient performance in extracting features from images,
while the transformer model captures both spatial features through self-attention and temporal
relationships through cross-attention, making it suitable for tasks like lip-audio synchroniza-
tion. Another strength is the use of cross-attention for detecting lip-audio synchronization
inconsistencies, a powerful indicator of manipulation since deepfakes often fail to synchronize
these two modalities.
Additionally, this research improves efficiency in processing videos by reducing the number of
frames from 300 to 30 in the video-only pipeline, as deepfakes typically persist throughout the
entire video. This approach helps to address computational costs. The audio-visual data fusion
using a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) also improves the model-centric manipulation detection
capability against attacks that affect only one of the modalities. Also, with cuboid embeddings,
since both space and time are fused simultaneously, spatiotemporal correlation is captured more
efficiently making the attention mechanisms easier to implement and learn these correlations
better.
Nonetheless, there are some potential drawbacks in this strategy. To begin with, the lip region
has been pre-processed where frames have converted into grey-scaled images. This may cause
loss of useful information which may involve lip movements or texture around the mouth re-
gion, which helps in the deepfake detection. Color pictures versus grayscale inputs have not
been tested or the results compared by the author, therefore the justification for this prepro-
cessing step is insufficient. In addition, the study does not address the model’s false positive or
false negative rates, thus making it challenging to ascertain what deepfakes would confuse the
model the most.
Another concern is the conversion of the audio signal from stereo to mono to reduce input size.
While this decreases computational complexity, it risks losing spatial audio cues, potentially
masking manipulations, especially if the deepfake is applied only to one channel of the audio.
Lastly, while the use of VGG-16 and two separate transformer encoders for the audio and video
pipelines is effective, it significantly increases computational complexity, which may hinder the
approach’s applicability for real-time detection. More efficient architectures may be necessary
for such applications.

2.1.5.1 Summary of the research item
In the paper "Lips Are Lying: Spotting the Temporal Inconsistency between Audio and Vi-
sual in Lip-Syncing DeepFakes"[99], the authors introduce LipFD, a novel framework designed
to detect lip-sync forgeries by analyzing discrepancies between audio and video in deepfake
technologies. The main goal of the LipFD framework is to provide a solution to the chal-
lenge of detecting lip synchronization errors in input videos that have been manipulated. The
architecture includes a Global Feature Encoder that incorporates self-attention to realize long
audio-visual relationships so as to address lip sync problems.
LipFD is made of three major modules: a Region Awareness module, which provides feature
weighting, hence enabling the model to concentrate on the most important parts of input. Also,
the architecture uses crop encoding that looks at wider portions of the input in order to carry
out lip motion more effectively. The authors implement LipFD testing against others’ lead-
ing methods and demonstrate that it achieves better performance across multiple datasets that
include the A VLips proposed dataset, FF++ and DFDC. Metrics such as the accuracy (ACC),
average precision (AP), false positive rate (FPR), and false negative rate (FNR) are utilized
to evaluate the efficiency of the model. Last but not least, the resilience of LipFD is evalu-
ated against popular perturbation attacks and is found to outperform contemporary deepfake
detection techniques.

3.2 Methodology
Our methodology emphasizes the synchronous processing of visual and auditory information.
We employ attention mechanisms to detect any discrepancies between lip movements and the
corresponding audio. The detailed steps are outlined below.
3.2.1 Input Video Stream
The input consists of a video stream enriched with the appropriate audio tracks. It is crucial
that the lip movements in the video correspond to the produced audio.
3.2.2 Pre-Processing
The following pre-processing procedures prepare the video and audio data for further analysis:
• Face Detection: Using the DLIB library[ 1111], we identify and extract faces from each
frame, isolating relevant portions of the facial structure.
• Feature Localization: We pinpoint key regions of interest, such as the mouth and cheeks,
to focus on the areas responsible for speech production.
• Audio Extraction: We utilize techniques like Mel Frequency Cepstral Coefficients (MFCC)[1212]
and Mel Spectrogram to capture the auditory features that correspond with lip move-
ments.
• Frame Alignment: We ensure that the audio and visual frames are synchronized, main-
taining a temporal correlation between the features extracted from both modalities.
3.2.3 Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for Lip Movement Feature
Extraction
After pre-processing, the data is passed to a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to extract
lip movement features. The CNN architecture comprises three convolutional layers with 32,
64, and 128 filters respectively. Each convolutional layer is followed by a max pooling layer
to reduce spatial dimensions and capture essential features. The extracted features are then
flattened into a one-dimensional feature vector, preparing them for subsequent analysis.

3. Proposed Method
3.2.4 Attention Mechanisms for Lip-Audio Synchronization
The flattened lip movement features are fed into the Lip-Audio Synchronization block, which
employs attention mechanisms to analyze both audio and lip features over time. This block
consists of:
• Self-Attention Layer: Focuses on the temporal dynamics of lip movements, capturing
dependencies across different time steps.
• Audio Self-Attention Layer: Extracts relevant audio features by attending to important
aspects of the audio signal over time.
• Cross-Attention Layer: Integrates lip and audio features, enhancing the synchronization
between lip movements and corresponding audio signals.
These attention layers work together to identify inconsistencies between lip movements and
audio, which are indicative of Deepfake manipulations.
3.2.5 CNN and Cross-Attention Layers for Lip-Cheek Muscle Movement
Correspondence
Parallel to lip-audio synchronization, the lip movement features are also processed in the Lip-
Cheek Muscle Movement Correspondence block. This block focuses on analyzing cheek mus-
cle movements over time through:
• Cheek Muscle CNN: A dedicated CNN extracts detailed features related to cheek mus-
cle movements from the video frames.
• Cross-Attention Layer: Combines the extracted cheek muscle features with the lip
movement features, allowing the model to correlate movements in both regions.
This integration helps in detecting subtle inconsistencies between lip and cheek movements,
further improving Deepfake detection accuracy.
3.2.6 Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) for Classification and Feature Fu-
sion
After processing through the attention mechanisms, the synchronized features from the Lip-
Audio Synchronization block are passed to a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) for initial classi-
fication. Simultaneously, the output from the Lip-Cheek Muscle Movement Correspondence
