Despite the wealth of literature on concept drift, existing approaches typically focus on
handling abrupt or gradual drifts adhering to the intuition that shifts in data distribution
occur abruptly or evolve gradually over time. In a few domains including supply chain,
the drifting of recurring concepts is a unique challenge. Recurring drift occurs when old
properties, patterns, or distributions are seen again and again, usually caused by cyclic
or seasonal things like seasonally changing consumer behavior around holiday sales or
predictable market conditions. But these drifts are not random; they are part of identifiable
cycles and so a detection mechanism must be adaptive, nuanced, and tuned to their time
scale.
However, these recurring drifts tend to be inadequately addressed by current detection
methodologies. Most approaches are reactive and discover drifts only after their occur-
rence, or they are proactive and can predict drifts but often at the computational and scal-
ability cost. The research problem, therefore, lies at the intersection of these challenges:
a real-time detection system can work with large-scale data streams, and yet be able to
account for repeating patterns that the traditional methods could miss out.
The consequences of failing to detect recurring drift are profound. Real-time decision-
making means that the models cannot adapt to such changes and can end up making out-
dated or wrong predictions which can result in large monetary losses in business settings.
An example is supply chain logistics where supply and demand seasonality is disregarded
leading to increased or decreased sales and supply line efficiency. The problem addressed
in this study sits within a must-refined urgent need for scalable, domain-specific solutions
to the complexity of concept drift with recurrence.
1.2 Research Problem Statement
At the core of this research is a fundamental question: Real-time detection and adapta-
tion to recurring concept drift in the context of streaming data streams? It notes that the
existing methods do not address the cyclical nature of many data patterns and this is a
significant gap in the current body of knowledge.
When we consider the growing reliance on streaming data in modern applications, the
problem becomes especially acute. With real-time collection of data, the ability to adapt
to changing data distributions is a key factor in keeping machine learning models accurate
and relevant. Statistical tests, drift detectors, or adaptive learning techniques that ensure
purity or speed tend to sacrifice either one of them in their large-scale, real-time systems.
In addition, most current methods are not designed to capture such recurrent drift and
therefore do not capture the nuances of periodically repeating patterns that are commonly
present in many practical situations.

evaluated on IoTID20 and NSL-KDD datasets which are popular in anomaly detection

2.1.2 Disposition-Based Concept Drift Detection and Adaptation in
Data Stream
2.1.2.1 Summary of the research item
The paper ( 22) introduces the Disposition-Based Concept Drift Detection Method (DB-
DDM) as a novel concept to enhance accuracy in detecting drift and the significant prob-
lem of false positive genuine drift. In data streams, concept drift means that in a stream
the distribution of data has changed resulting in wrong or outdated results from trained
learning models. The previous drift detection techniques are problematic in their ability
to distinguish between real drifts from noise and outliers. In such a case this often leads
to upgrading the models when it’s not necessary, which in turn compromises the quality
of the models.
DBDDM presented a two-window system that has an anchored window of old records and
a current window for newly received data. The method uses a statistical randomization
test wherein the significance of changes between the two windows is determined. DB-
DDM can then compare the distribution of the data in these windows to decide whether a
significant drift has occurred or not. If the drift is large, the model is calibrated; otherwise,
the system does not recognize the drift at all. By experiments with different synthesized
and real datasets, the paper proves that DBDDM can boost classifier accuracy by up to
28% with classifiers like Naive Bayes and Hoeffding Trees.
2.1.2.2 Critical analysis of the research item
One of the biggest strengths of DBDDM is that the method proposed by the authors aims
at the reduction of false positives regarding drift detection, which in turn contributes to
more stabilization of the models applied in the context of a changing environment. The
two-window comparison system also offers a more refined notion of changes in data,
which enables model updates only when a change a more appropriate analysis of data is
evident. By excluding noise and random oscillations, the holdout set prevents the model
from degrading in its performance with time and keeps its qualities valid.

fixed-size two-window mechanism may not be very effective for a fast-changing environ-

2.1.3 Learning in the Presence of Concept Recurrence in Data Stream

2.1.3.1 Summary of the research item
(33) aims at responding to an important concern related to concept recurrence in data
stream clustering namely, how clusters that have been learned by the system can recur
in consecutive data streams. It is most relevant to several real-life situations that are evi-
dent from the ability of patterns to be repetitive as influenced by events and occurrences in
the external environment like consumer behavior analysis and trends in operations of the
financial markets to mention but a few. The authors present an algorithm that is intended
to recognize these similar ideas more effectively and more rapidly through employing
models that have been learned and archived before.
As shown in 2.22.2, the algorithm is active in two stages that are quite distinct in their pro-
cesses. The first one is an online phase which is concerned with calculating summary for
the accumulating data points and the second one is an offline phase where macro-clusters
are produced from the summarized data points. In the online phase, necessary statistics
as linear combinations and sums of squares are obtained and saved for later use. Thus,
new data entering the system will be classified and integrated into the proper models that
represent previously learned notions. The algorithm proves to increase efficiency and
decrease the degree of computational complexity achieved by retrieving these models as
opposed to training new ones from the ground up. To support their claims, the authors
employ a synthetic dataset and two real-world datasets to show not only the capability of
their approach in identifying recurring concepts but also the lesser space required as well
as the fewer computations needed compared to other approaches.
This idea is based on the belief that data streams are usually non-stationary; in other
words, the distribution of the data might change with time. This characteristic makes
traditional clustering methods unsuitable since these algorithms do not take into consid-
eration the repetition of previously learned concepts. To this end, focusing on this gap,
the paper stresses the need to design algorithms to track and adapt prior knowledge to
enhance real-time performance.

2.1.4.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The NACRE framework is a substantial improvement over the existing state of the art
for conceptual drift detection in non-stationary domains. Its greatest asset is the online
update, whereby the system can update itself without a major shift from the model. De-
pending on the changes in the concepts, this feature allows avoiding sharp transitions be-
tween the concepts and, therefore, mitigating the performance degradation during drifts.
The framework uses a recurrent drift classifier and apply the PEARL method that com-
bines different types of trees, including the foreground tree, the background tree, and the
candidate tree to observe the recurrent drifts and increase the accuracy of the prediction.
Furthermore, the incorporation of a drift sequence predictor enables future drifts to be
forecasted depending on intervals in the past, which makes the system accurate in the long
run. The evaluation of the parameters of the framework shows that the values are stable,
which implies that there will be little effect on the performance in stable data stream so
making deployment and tuning easier. However, there are some weaknesses associated
with the NACRE framework as well. This is because its implementation involves several
components; namely the drift classifier, predictor, and coordinator.
Moreover, the accuracy of the drift sequence predictor is highly dependent on the past
drift data; if the information available is inadequate or not very relevant, then the predic-
tions developed will not be very sound. The framework may also be prone to overfitting
especially in environment with high fluctuations of data streams since specific models are
difficult to adapt to new situations, Therefore, one concludes that, although the NACRE
framework offers a potential solution for the proactive detection of drift, the directions
for further improvement have been also identified. Integrating meta-information into the
drift coordinator and making the coordinator capable of tuning itself to the characteristics
of different data streams are the next logical steps to improve the drift coordinator. These
improvements will not only fill the existing gaps but also will bring the framework to the
optimum level of development. Thus, the focus on these improvements presupposes the
further enhancement and flexibility of the NACRE framework as the tool for combating
concept drift in a number of applications.

2.1.5 Recurrent Adaptive Classifier Ensemble for Handling Recur-
ring Concept Drifts
2.1.5.1 Summary of the research item
The work of Museba et al. (2021) ( 55) introduces the Recurrent Adaptive Classifier En-
semble (RACE) which is a new approach to dealing with recurring concept drifts in real-
world data streams. RACE addresses the concept drift in terms of data distribution since
the data distribution may change over time, and RACE is directed to recurring concepts,
which can appear in cycles.
RACE employs Support Vector Machines (SVMs) as base models, with an archive of
previously learned models of diverse types to quickly transition based on new data, while
retaining the learned knowledge. In the case of the arrival of a new data chunk, RACE
is used to assess whether this data chunk follows the distribution of the previous data.
If there is a change, the current support vector is modified, and a new support vector is
procured.
Unlike conventional methods, where mean predictions are obtained from stored models,
RACE fine tunes these models to the data available today thus providing better accuracy.
The algorithm targets diversification of models and uses Yule’s Q Statistic in order to mit-
igate errors in the ensemble. By taking this approach on account of diversity, RACE is
able to learn more efficiently and with better precision the recurring concepts with low
computational cost. Quantitative experiments on synthetic and real applications demon-

3.2 Key features of the proposed approach
Several key features are built into DriftGuard to improve its ability to detect and han-
dle concept drift. The biggest feature is its anticipatory drift detection mechanism that
predicts possible drifts based on historical data patterns in the stream. DriftGuard uses a
Temporal Pattern Graph to analyze past drift occurrences to find correlations and facilitate
proactive adaptation before drift negatively impacts predictive accuracy. This capability
allows the system to anticipate drift instead of just reacting to them, which improves sta-
bility in overall performance.
Also, handling multi-modal drift is another crucial feature that is often present in such
complex data spaces. It allows DriftGuard to maintain and adapt multiple models in par-
allel in response to a combination of simultaneous changes in various subsets of monitored
data. This becomes critical when many factors affect various elements of performance,
specifically in scenarios where influence on demand and logistical constraints, while the
same, affect different areas differently.
Existing frameworks only replace models when they detect drift, while DriftGuard allows
incremental model refinement. Hence, rather than discarding the models altogether, the
framework can continue to revise existing models according to new incoming data. This
hybrid approach allows the system to learn and adapt while maintaining its inherent per-
formance levels. DriftGuard also uses an adaptive forgetting mechanism to manage the
size of the Tree Transition Graph. With this mechanism, we can retain critical transitions
associated with recurring drifts while removing less significant edges, preserving valuable
historical information that can inform future adaptations.

3.3 Visualization of the Dashboard
An important element of this research framework is the work with a dashboard, as the
visualization tool used for the monitoring and analysis of the drift of concepts in real-
time streams. This dashboard allows stakeholders to easily comprehend visualizations of
how drift affects the model and more crucially how a repeated pattern in datasets may
necessitate a redoing of new models to remain accurate.
3.3.1 Purpose of the Dashboard
The first and foremost purpose of the dashboard is to provide the concept drift of the data
streams with periodic patterns that occur during the process. In supply chain management
and other such fields where large amounts and complex data are being generated, the
process of manually identifying these patterns can be extremely onerous and prone to
error due to the sheer scale and intensity of information being received. Metrics calculated
by the dashboard combined with the Ex collection make it easy for the users to analyze
values and check for recurring shifts that are not visible with the standard approach.
Although such a framework can be used for a variety of use cases within the research, the
proposed dashboard is equally versatile with regards to data streams that can be visualised.
In the context of this section an example visualization based on the comparison of the
sales and shipping performance through several years is provided. This example is one
single instance and can be taken to other areas, where operational metrics are involved, be
it inventory stock, supplier’s delivery hours, or customer demand pattern, making it very
flexible for the supply chain and e-commerce environments.
One of the visualizations 3.23.2 created for the dashboard is that showing sales and shipping
performances over several years and will allow investigating the impact of a drift of the
concept in these indicators. The specific example deals with considering patterns of re-
peated drift that may be observed in the course of particular months during several years.
The data set includes monthly data for the total sales for three years from 2015 to 2017,
and average shipping days. The visualization annexed hereto shows that there is a cycle of
declines in sales in the second month of each year, February 2015, February 2016, Febru-
ary 2017. This decline in sales points to a consistent shift, and this happens on a yearly
basis, during the same time of the year. This recurring behaviour can only imply that push
or pull factors characteristic with the supply chain negatively impact on the sales during
this particular period. Such tendencies can be, for instance, disconnected with the de-
crease in the demand during holidays, transportation slowdowns, or other reasons which
led to cycle decline. Each of these patterns is vividly displayed on the dashboard so that
the user can easily appreciate the importance of these repeat slants.
