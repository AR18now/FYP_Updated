1. Introduction
1.2 Scope
The scope of the Humrahi carpooling application centers on creating a practical, effi-
cient, and user-friendly ride-sharing platform aimed at urban commuters and university
students. The application will feature advanced route-matching algorithms to optimize
user convenience and match drivers and passengers based on proximity, destination, and
schedule. A dynamic pricing model will ensure fair cost distribution, adjusting based on
distance, demand, and timing. To prioritize user safety, Humrahi will implement real-time
vehicle tracking and a range of security measures. Recognizing cultural sensitivities, the
application will include features specifically supporting conservative female passengers,
such as female-only ride options and enhanced privacy settings.
The initial deployment will focus on Islamabad and its adjacent urban areas, where the
density of commuters makes carpooling both feasible and impactful. Expansion into ru-
ral areas and integration with public transportation are outside the scope, allowing the
project to focus on high-density urban regions. The development approach will include a
structured launch followed by iterative updates based on user feedback, helping address
emerging needs and refining the app’s usability. Budget limitations and technological
constraints will shape feature prioritization, scalability, and real-time data management.
Ethical considerations, especially around data privacy and security, will be paramount,
ensuring compliance with relevant regulations and safeguarding user information. By
addressing the diverse needs of urban commuters through innovative features, Humrahi
seeks to enhance commuting efficiency, reduce transportation costs, and promote sustain-
able transportation solutions while fostering inclusivity, security, and user comfort.

1.4 User Classes and Characteristics
User class Description
Commuters Commuters represent the primary users of the Humrahi carpooling app, con-
sisting of urban dwellers who regularly travel to workplaces or educational
institutions. This group includes university students and office workers who
seek an affordable and efficient transportation solution. These users priori-
tize convenience, cost savings, and safety. A majority of them are expected
to book rides daily, using the app during peak commuting hours.
Drivers Drivers are individuals who own vehicles and are willing to share their ride
with other commuters traveling along similar routes. This class includes
private vehicle owners, university students with vehicles, and working pro-
fessionals who want to offset their travel costs by carpooling. Drivers need
to interact with features like ride offer creation, route selection, and real-
time notifications, and they’ll be able to view commuter profiles and ratings
for safety.
Female
Commuters
This class specifically targets female users who seek carpooling options tai-
lored to safety and privacy concerns. Female commuters may include stu-
dents and working women who prioritize female-only rides. The app offers
unique features, like female-only ride options and enhanced privacy settings,
designed to make commuting safer and more comfortable for this group.
Adminsitrators Administrators are responsible for managing the carpooling application’s
functionality and safety compliance. Their primary tasks include oversee-
ing user accounts and verification processes, monitoring ride requests, and
ensuring adherence to safety protocols. They moderate content to prevent
inappropriate behavior, analyze user data to inform strategic decisions, and
provide support for user queries and complaints. Additionally, they gener-
ate reports on user engagement and safety incidents to ensure transparency
and accountability. By centralizing these responsibilities, App Administra-
tors play a crucial role in maintaining an efficient and secure ride-sharing
platform.

3.1 Architectural Design
For Humrahi, we can structure the system into a 3-layer architecture, each with distinct
roles and responsibilities:
3.1.0.1 1. Presentation Layer
Description: The Presentation Layer is the user-facing part of the system, handling all
interactions between users (commuters and drivers) and the application. It provides the
interface where users can log in, register, search for rides, view route details, and make
payments. This layer is built using technologies that support web and mobile responsive-
ness for a seamless user experience.
3.1.0.2 2. Business Logic Layer
Description: The Business Logic Layer is the heart of the system, where all business logic
and decision-making happen. It connects the front end and the database layers, processing
user requests and managing the system’s core functionalities like ride matching, payment
processing, and route optimization. It enforces the rules for how users and data interact.
3.1.0.3 3. Data Layer
The Data Layer manages the storage, retrieval, and updating of all persistent data. It stores
information about users, rides, payments, and logs. This layer ensures data integrity,
security, and availability to the application layer whenever needed.

3.3 Data Design
3.3.0.1 1. User Profiles
• Structure: Each user profile contains the following fields:
– user_id: Unique identifier for each user.
– name: Full name of the user.
– email: User’s email address.
– phone: User’s phone number.
– user_type: Whether the user is a passenger or a driver.
– vehicle_info (for drivers): Information about the driver’s vehicle, including
make, model, and license plate.
– ratings: A record of the average user rating.
• Processing: User profiles are created during registration. Users can manually up-
date their profile details. The system also processes user ratings based on completed
rides, allowing drivers and passengers to rate each other.
• Storage: User profiles are stored in MongoDB, which allows for a flexible schema
to support the differences between passenger and driver profiles.
3.3.0.2 2. Ride Requests
• Structure: Ride request entities store information regarding a passenger’s request
for a ride:
– ride_request_id: Unique identifier for each ride request.
– passenger_id: Reference to the passenger making the request.
– origin: Pickup location of the ride.
– destination: Drop-off location of the ride.
– status: Current status of the request (e.g., pending, matched, completed).
• Processing: The system processes new ride requests, matches them to available
drivers, and updates the status as the ride progresses. When matched, the ride re-
quest links to a specific driver and matched ride entity.
• Storage: Ride requests are stored in MongoDB, with references to both the user
and driver involved, making it easy to track the lifecycle of a ride.
37
3. System Overview
3.3.0.3 3. Drivers
• Structure: Driver entities represent the drivers who offer rides to passengers:
– driver_id: Unique identifier for each driver.
– vehicle_info: Vehicle details including make, model, and license plate.
– ratings: A history of driver ratings from passengers.
• Processing: Drivers are associated with one or more ride requests. Their ratings
are dynamically updated after completing rides, providing feedback to other users.
• Storage: Driver data is stored in MongoDB, linked to the ride requests and matched
rides they are associated with.
3.3.0.4 4. Matched Rides
• Structure: This entity represents a ride that has been successfully matched between
a passenger and a driver:
– ride_id: Unique identifier for the matched ride.
– ride_request_id: Reference to the originating ride request.
– driver_id: Reference to the driver who accepted the ride request.
– status: Current status of the ride (e.g., ongoing, completed).
• Processing: Once a ride request is matched to a driver, this entity is created. The
system updates the ride status as it progresses and manages payment transactions
associated with the ride.
• Storage: Matched rides are stored in MongoDB, with references to both the driver
and the passenger.
3.3.0.5 5. Payments
• Structure: Payment entities capture the details of transactions for completed rides:
– payment_id: Unique identifier for the payment.
– ride_id: Reference to the matched ride associated with the payment.
– total_amount: Total fare amount calculated for the ride.
– status: Status of the payment (e.g., pending, completed).

3.3 Data Design
• Processing: The system calculates the fare based on the distance traveled and dy-
namic pricing algorithms. It processes payments after the ride is completed, ensur-
ing that both the passenger and driver have completed the trip.
• Storage: Payment data is stored in MongoDB, with references to the matched ride
and user.
3.3.0.6 6. Ride Reviews
• Structure: Ride reviews allow passengers and drivers to provide feedback on each
other:
– review_id: Unique identifier for the review.
– ride_id: Reference to the associated ride.
– user_id: Reference to the user who left the review.
– rating: Numeric rating (1-5).
– comments: Optional text field for additional feedback.
• Processing: The system enables users to leave reviews after completing a ride.
Ratings are aggregated for drivers and passengers to maintain an average score.
• Storage: Ride reviews are stored in MongoDB, linked to the user and the matched
ride.
3.3.0.7 7. Pricing Analytics
• Structure: Pricing analytics are used to dynamically calculate fares based on fac-
tors such as distance, time, and demand:
– analytics_id: Unique identifier for the pricing analytics data.
– ride_id: Reference to the associated ride.
– fare_components: Breakdown of various factors contributing to the fare
(e.g., base fare, surge pricing, discounts).
• Processing: The system uses pricing analytics to adjust fares in real-time, taking
into account route, demand, and ride conditions.
• Storage: Pricing analytics are stored in MongoDB, associated with matched rides
for fare calculation and auditing.
