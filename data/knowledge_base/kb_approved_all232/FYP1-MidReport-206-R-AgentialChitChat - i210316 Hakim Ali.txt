2.1.1 Quasi-Equivalence Discovery [ 11]
In Quasi-Equivalence Discovery (QED) for zero-shot communication (ZSC), quasi-equivalence
refers to the identification of symmetries in communication protocols. Symmetries refer
to situations where different actions, messages, or sequences of actions lead to the same
outcome or result. These symmetries allow agents to generalize their learned communi-
cation strategies to independently trained agents, even in zero-shot scenarios where the
agents have not been trained together.
2.1.1.1 Summary of the research item
Quasi-Equivalence Discovery (QED), iteratively discovers equivalence mappings between
optimal policies in multi-agent systems. Unlike traditional self-play , where agents co-
adapt during training, QED focuses on finding policies that generalizes on different agents.
The algorithm first identify symmetries by training agents with different policies and ob-
serving how different action or message sequences can achieve the same result. It does
this by comparing the behavior of multiple agents and finding equivalent policies that lead
to the same goal. After identifying these equivalences, QED trains new sets of policies
based on these symmetry mappings. This allows the agents to communicate success-
fully with novel agents, because they can recognize the symmetries and generalize their

2.1.2 Multi-lingual agents through multi-headed neural networks [ 44]
In the paper, the authors maintained multiple heads in the neural network architecture for
each agent, where each head corresponds to a distinct communication language that the
agent learns with different partners.
2.1.2.1 Summary of the research item
Both the speaker and listener agents are modeled using deep neural networks. The core
idea is to assign a separate output head for each possible conversational partner. The
shared layers of the neural network are used for feature extraction. It is assumed that each
agent when communicating with other agent have full knowledge about their identity.
The heads are isolated from each other, meaning when training occurs with one partner,
only the corresponding head is updated, preventing other heads from being affected from
catastrophic forgetting.
2.1.2.2 Critical analysis of the research item
A key limitation noted by the authors is that the number of languages grows quadratically
with the number of agents. If many agents are involved, maintaining separate heads for
each one may lead to memory and computational overhead, not allowing scalability. Fur-
thermore, the method assumes that agents know who their conversational partner is, which

2.1.3 Towards Human-Agent Communication via the Information
Bottleneck Principle [66]
In traditional emergent communication, which focuses on optimizing task-specific util-
ity, can lead to communication that is either too complex for humans to understand or
inefficient in terms of convergence rates. To address this, they explore how a trade off be-
tween utility, informativeness, and complexity can help agents develop more human-like
communication.
2.1.3.1 Summary of the research item
The goal of paper is to create an emergent communication system where agents not only
maximize task performance but also optimize the informativeness and complexity of their
communication signals.
They are using information bottleneck framework to balance the trade off between in-
formation and complexity of messages. For this they are using VQ-VIB, that combines
elements from vector quantized variational autoencoders (VQ-V AE) and B-variational
autoencoders,to compress and discretize communication signals. The overall loss func-
tion includes terms for utility, informativeness, and complexity. The agents are trained to
maximize this objective function.
2.1.3.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Paper explores another dimension in emergence of communication between multi agent
systems where the emergence is inspired by human like communication for effective and
better convergence. Experiments of these papers indeed show better convergence than
vanilla methods for emergence of communication.

2.1.4 Emergent Discrete Communication in Semantic Spaces [ 55]
This paper explores how agents can develop discrete communication tokens embedded in
a continuous semantic space, which improves both task performance and flexibility while
also supporting human understanding.
2.1.4.1 Summary of the Research Item
The paper trains agents to develop discrete communication tokens within a continuous
semantic space to communicate effectively in multi-agent tasks. The sender uses a fixed
set of tokens learned during training, but these tokens capture semantic relationships,
allowing the agents to generalize and achieve zero-shot communication.

but differs by placing greater emphasis on language transfer between agents.

Proposed Approach
There has been significant progress in the field of agents learning to develop languages for
communication to accomplish tasks. However, the challenge of zero-shot communication
remains largely unaddressed. While some studies have attempted to solve this problem,
the majority focus on policy learning rather than actual language transfer. One notable pa-
per utilized quasi-equivalence to tackle this issue, but a direct method for teaching agents
a shared language for zero-shot communication is still lacking, as quasi-equivalence needs
to be clearly defined before enabling effective agent interaction.
Objective
Our proposed approach focuses on developing a common language that allows agents to
first create their own language and then transfer this language to other agents. This will be
achieved by analyzing how agents communicate their ideas and send messages to convey
their plans effectively.
Methodology
To implement this, we will introduce a penalty term in the loss function, leveraging a
trained agent to guide the learning process. This penalty will encourage agents to adhere
to a communication protocol, ensuring they convey their ideas in a consistent language
rather than random messages.

3. Proposed Approach
Policy Independence
The goal is to maintain policy independence while establishing a shared communication
protocol. Our algorithm will enable a teacher agent to critique a child agent based on the
policy the new agent is following, rather than solely relying on the original policy it was
trained on.
Significance
This approach aims to facilitate meaningful communication among agents in zero-shot
scenarios, enhancing their collaborative capabilities while preserving their individual learn-
ing processes. By developing a robust framework for language transfer, we hope to bridge
the gap between policy learning and effective communication, thereby advancing multi-
agent systems.
