1.2 Research Problem Statement
The primary research problem, which this study attempts to address, involves improving
methods for extracting, interpreting, and representing causal relationships from large-
scale news datasets with the help of detailed causal knowledge graphs. Although the
current methods proposed by Bhatia, A., et al. (2023) [11] and Gopalakrishnan, S., et al.
(2023) [44] have proved their abilities to detect causal relations, they fail to produce robust,
large-scale causal graphs that represent multiple cause-effect pairs across an entire news
article in one pass.
The recent breakthroughs in using large language models(LLMs) for causal reasoning, as
presented in Bhatia, A., et al. (2023) [11] and Susanti, Y., et al. (2024) [1212], show promise
to advance causal understanding. However, the lack of structured output and accuracy
in these models becomes a barrier for their practical uses in real-world applications. A
significant shortcoming of current approaches is their reliance on sentence-level classifi-
cation that fails to capture the full complexity in the structure of news headlines. Causal
relations in news frequently span across multiple sentences, paragraphs, or even articles,
leading to an incomplete understanding of the relationships between events.
The method described in the paper Causal Reasoning in Large Language Models using
Causal Graph Retrieval Augmented Generation (2024) [1111] shows promise by incorpo-
rating causal graphs into the generation process using Retrieval-Augmented Generation
(RAG). However, this approach has some limitations, such as a fixed depth in retriev-
ing causal context, which restricts its ability to capture long-term cause-and-effect con-
nections. Additionally, relying on summarization models to divide the text into smaller
pieces can be challenging because poor chunking might leave out important causal links.
Our research aims to address the following key challenges:
• How can we enhance the extraction of multiple cause-and-effect relationships from
short, condensed news headlines using advanced NLP techniques and Causal Graph
RAG?
• How can the integration of Causal Graph RAG techniques help in resolving ambigu-
ities when extracting causal relationships from headlines with multiple cause-effect
pairs?
• How can we ensure that the cause-and-effect relationships found in news headlines
are organized into a clear and easy-to-understand causal graphs?
• How can LLMs be fine-tuned to improve the extraction of interconnected causal
relationships, especially in complex news headlines?

2.1.6 Event Causality Extraction via Implicit Cause-Effect Interac-

2.1.6.1 Summary of the research item
The research paper, Event Causality Extraction via Implicit Cause-Effect Interactions
by Liu, J., et al. [99], focuses on extracting cause-effect pairs from text to improve mod-
els’ ability to understand event causality. This is known as Event Causality Extraction
(ECE), it requires advanced reasoning to recognize implicit interactions between cause
and effect events. The paper introduces an Implicit Cause-Effect Interaction (ICE)
framework, which identifies cause and effect relationships by using templates for con-
ditional generation. It helps the system predict causes or effects based on context, even
when they aren’t explicitly mentioned. The model uses additional information (i.e event
types and arguments) during training to improve its reasoning skills. A major innova-
tion in this paper is the use ofknowledge distillation technique, where two teacher models
transfer their knowledge to a student model. This allows the student model to perform
well even when the additional information is not available during testing.
The ICE framework also introduces a new alignment strategy called Cause-Effect Op-
timal Transport (CEOT) , which enhances the relationship between cause and effect
events. This ensures that model captures both intra- and inter- event interactions, improv-
ing the extractions of event structures. Testing on the ECE-CCKS dataset showed that
the ICE framework outperformed previous methods, achieving an 8.39% increase in F1-
score. This improvement demonstrates the model’s ability to identify implicit causal rela-
tionships, making it effective for tasks like machine reading comprehension and predict-
ing future events. The study successfully overcomes limitations in existing ECE models
that rely mostly on classification methods. By switching to a generation-based approach
and adding mechanisms to use additional information, the ICE framework improves the
models ability to understand causality.
2.1.6.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The paper offers significant contributions to the field of causal reasoning in natural lan-
guage processing. The ICE framework moves beyond traditional classification approaches,
which often fail to capture complex cause-effect relationships. By introducing a template-
based generative approach and leveraging additional information during training, the au-
thors ensure that the model develops a deep understanding of both intra- and inter- causal
relationships. This methodology increases the model’s capability to extract causal rela-
tionships more accurately, leading to state-of the art performance on benchmark datasets.
However, the reliance on additional information (i.e event types and arguments) is ef-

2.1.8 CHEER: Centrality-aware high-order event reasoning network
for document-level event causality identification
2.1.8.1 Summary of the research item
The paper titled CHEER: Centrality-aware High-order Event Reasoning Network for
Document-level Event Causality Identification by Chen et al. [22] focuses on improving
how we identify cause-and-effect relationships between events in documents. This task,
called Document-level Event Causality Identification (DECI), is difficult because events
are spread out across the document, and there aren’t always clear words linking causes
and effects between sentences.
18
2.1 Related Research
The authors introduce a model called CHEER (Centrality-aware High-order Event
Reasoning network), which uses graph neural networks (GNNs) to reason about these
events. CHEER builds an Event Interaction Graph (EIG) , showing how events and
event pairs interact. A key aspect in CHEER is that it factors in the centrality of events
during its reasoning, treating central events as key anchors to understand cause-effect
relationships in the document. By labeling central events in datasets (such as EventSto-
ryLine), the authors created features that capture the role and importance of these events.
CHEER performs better than existing models on two benchmark datasets, showing that it
is more effective in identifying causal relationships in documents.
2.1.8.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The CHEER framework presents an innovative approach to addressing the limitations
of traditional DECI models by considering event centrality. Previous models such as
RichGCN and ERGO focused on constructing document-level graphs for cross-sentence
reasoning but failed to incorporate the hierarchical importance of certain events. CHEER’s
ability to integrate both single-event nodes and event-pair nodes in its Event Interaction
Graph (EIG) enables it to perform high-order reasoning, capturing transitive causal rela-
tionships. This approach aligns well with scenarios where certain central events influence
most other events in a document.
The research does have certain limitations. While the integrating event centrality en-
hances model performance, the prediction of central events is not entirely accurate. The
authors acknowledge that the centrality prediction can be further improved using more
sophisticated encoding methods. Moreover, the reliance on manually annotated central
events raises concerns about scalability for larger datasets. In future work, automating
this annotation process could handle this issue. Despite these limitations, CHEER in-
troduces a valuable improvement over existing methods by systematically addressing the
lack of structured causal information at the document level.
2.1.8.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The CHEER model focuses on understanding how events are connected and which events
are the most important. This focus is very relevant to our research NewsGuardX, which
aims to understand complicated causal relationships in news. In this context, being able
to create causal graphs and check the reasoning behind event sequences is essential for
making news headlines more reliable and transparent. The centrality-aware framework in
CHEER can be adapted to find key events in news articles, which would help in verifying
causal claims more effectively.
Additionally, CHEER’s use of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) for complex reasoning

monitor and ensure the quality of generated headlines. By including event centrality, the

2.1.9 Causal knowledge extraction from long text maintenance doc-
uments
2.1.9.1 Summary of the research item
Causal Knowledge Extraction from Long Text Maintenance Documents" deals with the
extraction of causal knowledge fromWork Request Notification (WRN)records, which
is brought into practice on the manufacturing lines of nearly every industry for tracking
cases of equipment failure and maintenance-related activities. Generally, such high vol-
umes of unstructured text occurring in these records are very tough to analyze and inter-
pret at human levels. The authors of this paper discuss a two-stage deep learning pipeline
with the specific aim of extracting meaningful cause-and-effect information from these
WRNs.
The first stage involves sentence-level noise removal, filtering unwanted information. This
stage is of significance for WRNs because they typically contain information not relevant
to the causal relations - just administrative notes or nonspecific observations. The second
stage employs a transformer-based model so that entities and their relationships can be
extracted jointly. In fact, it most often emphasizes causal triples consisting of the cause,
effect, and their relation. A Causal Graph Database is constructed and then queried by
the technical staff, identifying patterns of equipment failure and supporting appropriate
asset-management decisions. The whole evaluation is done over the dataset of 98,000
WRN records; with achieving high F1 score (83% and 92% for cause and effect re-
spectively) for both entity recognition and relation extraction, the system’s success on
large-scale real-world data is justified.
2.1.9.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Research has successfully overcome the challenge of extracting causal knowledge from
long, unstructured texts in industrial maintenance records. Novelty to the approach of
sentence-level noise removal is a strength in itself here because it tends to simplify text
data for better information extraction. Not surprisingly, applying deep learning models
and using BERT to classify noise and SpERT for entity-relation extraction points toward

2.1 Related Research
the applicability of latest NLP techniques to such a narrow yet technological area. The
study emphasizes the role of pre-processing in other areas, like jargon, abbreviations, and
spelling inconsistency, and all these are relevant to making the credibility of causal infor-
mation extracted. It makes it possible to construct a causal graph database on which users
can query and picture causality between failures of equipment and their consequences,
adding practicality to the focus.
There is an important limitation. The quality of the noise removal stage depends heav-
ily on the existence of a corresponding annotated training dataset, which requires much
expert effort. This also then would also pose challenges to scaling the approach to other
domains or maintenance environments where domain experts are not available. While
impressive results are obtained in the intra-sentential causal relations extraction, the inter-
sentential causality may be more deserving of more study. Further limitation lies in being
dependent on exact matching in the graph database, which may fail to generalize over sim-
ilar but lexically varied failure descriptions. It must be mentioned that the system exposi-
tions very high performance on this specific industrial context; however, open questions
arise in portability to domains which include unstructured data.
