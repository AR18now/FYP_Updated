2.1 Related Research
2.1.1 Deep Fake Detection and Classification Using Error-Level
Analysis and Deep Learning
2.1.1.1 Summary of the research item
This paper by Rimsha Rafique et al. [ 77] talks about the growing threat posed by deep
fake technology, which creates manipulated media that can spread misinformation. The
authors propose an automated method for detecting and classifying deep fake images by
combining error-level analysis (ELA) with deep learning techniques. To begin, ELA is
used to identify any pixel-level alterations in images, which are then processed using con-
volutional neural networks (CNNs) for deep feature extraction. The extracted features are
then classified using Support Vector Machines (SVM) and K-nearest Neighbors (KNN),
achieving the highest accuracy of 89.5 percent with the ResNet architecture and KNN.
The results conclude that the proposed framework is efficient and robust, making it a vi-
able solution for real-time deep fake detection and classification implementation, which
is increasingly necessary in the age of social media.

2.1.2 Diffusion Models Beat GANs on Image Synthesis
2.1.2.1 Summary of the research item
The paper "Diffusion Models Beat GANs on Image Synthesis" [22] looks at how diffusion
models do comparatively a better job than GANs at creating high-quality images. Diffu-
sion models work by slowly removing noise from data to build images, which allows the
training process to be more stable and results in more diverse images than GANs. The
authors provide evidence showing that diffusion models score higher on evaluation met-
rics such as Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), proving they’re more effective for making
images.
2.1.2.2 Critical analysis of the research item
The paper brings forth the advantages of diffusion models; how they do not face the com-
mon problems that we have with GANs, such as mode collapse and unstable training.
There are many suggestive tests in the paper that show diffusion models produce better
images. But they lack the real-time capabilities as diffusion models require a lot of com-
puting power. There is also a gamble between image quality and speed because diffusion
models take longer to train. In spite of that, this paper is a big step forward in generative
modeling.

2.1.5 CIFAKE: Image Classification And Explainable Identification
Of AI-Generated Synthetic Images
2.1.5.1 Summary of the research item
The paper titled CIFAKE: Image Classification and Explainable Identification of AI-
Generated Synthetic Images [11] presents a study focused on enhancing our ability to
differentiate between real-life photographs and AI-generated images using computer vi-
sion techniques. The researchers create a synthetic dataset, CIFAKE, that mirrors the ten
classes of the CIFAR-10 dataset, generated via latent diffusion models (LDMs), providing
a binary classification problem to distinguish real images from synthetic ones. The classi-
fication is performed using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), which, after tuning
36 different architectures, achieved an accuracy of 92.98% in identifying AI-generated
images. The study also introduces Explainable AI (XAI) through Gradient Class Activa-
tion Mapping (Grad-CAM) to visualize the decision-making process of the CNN, reveal-
ing that minor background imperfections are more significant in detecting AI-generated

2.1.6 Faster Than Lies: Real-Time Deepfake Detection using Binary
Neural Networks
2.1.6.1 Summary of the research item
The paper "Faster Than Lies: Real-Time Deepfake Detection using Binary Neural Net-
works" [88] presents a novel approach to deepfake detection by utilizing Binary Neural
Networks (BNNs) to enhance both accuracy and efficiency. The research emphasizes the
need for real-time deepfake detection on low-resource devices like mobile phones and
personal computers. BNNs, which quantize weights and activations to 1-bit values, are
key in reducing the computational complexity of deepfake detection models. The pa-
per incorporates techniques like Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) and Local Binary Pattern
(LBP) to augment input features and improve the model’s ability to detect manipulation
artifacts in the frequency and texture domains. Experiments conducted on datasets such
as COCOFake, DFFD, and CIFAKE demonstrate that the proposed BNN-based method
achieves state-of-the-art performance while reducing computational consumption by up
to 20× compared to traditional deep learning models.

The study successfully tackles the challenge of achieving real-time deepfake detection by
leveraging BNNs, which offer significant memory and computational savings. This makes

gration of FFT and LBP further improves the model’s robustness by targeting frequency-

2.1.8 PromptMagician: Interactive Prompt Engineering for Text-to-
Image Creation
2.1.8.1 Summary of the research
The paper presentsPromptMagician [33], a visual analysis system designed to assist users
in refining and improving prompts for generative text-to-image models . While these
models, such as those based on diffusion techniques, have become popular for creating
high-quality images from natural language prompts, crafting effective prompts remains a
challenge due to the inherent complexity and ambiguity of natural language.
PromptMagician addresses this issue by integrating a prompt recommendation model
that takes a user’s initial prompt and retrieves similar prompt-image pairs from a large-
scale database called DiffusionDB. It identifies "special" keywords—those that are both
important and relevant to the user’s intent—to help refine the prompt further. The system
introduces a multi-level visualization to display the cross-modal embeddings of the re-
trieved images and recommended keywords, enabling users to explore and refine prompts
interactively.
2.1.8.2 Critical analysis of the research
The research paper on PromptMagician provides an essential solution by addressing the
challenge of effective prompt engineering. The proposed solution integrates a recommen-
dation model with interactive visual analysis, which enhances user experience and allows
users to generate high quality images.
The paper shows huge improvement over previous approach to prompt refinement, how-
ever, the system is primarily evaluated on the DiffusionDB dataset. While the dataset is
comprehensive, PromptMagician does not explore other generative text-to-image mod-
els or datasets. Furthermore the paper does not provide latency issues and computational
efficiency problems that may rise when dealing with larger dataset. Other limitations
are ambiguity in Keyword Importance (may lose context of data), dependence on
pre-existing data(may restrict the system’s ability to cater to highly unique prompts).
Addressing the above mentioned limitations can improve the system’s applicability and
impact on both research and practical use cases in creative fields.
