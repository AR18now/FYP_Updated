Chapter 1
Introduction
Diabetic Retinopathy (DR) is a leading cause of preventable blindness in diabetic patients.
Early detection and classification of DR stages are critical to prevent vision loss. With ad-
vancements in deep learning, automated systems for DR detection using retinal fundus images
have shown promise in enhancing the efficiency and accuracy of diagnosis.
1.1 Problem Domain
Traditional methods for diagnosing Diabetic Retinopathy rely on manual examination of retinal
images by specialists, which can be subjective and time-consuming. Convolutional Neural Net-
works (CNNs) have shown effectiveness in automating this process by extracting local features
from retinal images. However, CNNs are limited in capturing global dependencies in the im-
age, which are essential for understanding complex retinal structures. On the other hand, Vision
Transformers (ViTs) excel in capturing long-range dependencies but require large datasets and
computational resources, making them less practical for medical imaging tasks with limited
data availability.
1.2 Research Problem Statement
The challenge is to develop an efficient and accurate system for Diabetic Retinopathy detection
that leverages the strengths of both CNNs and ViTs. Specifically, how can a hybrid CNN-ViT
model be designed to enhance feature extraction and classification accuracy for DR detec-
tion, while addressing the limitations of each individual architecture? This research aims to
investigate the viability of such a hybrid model and its effectiveness in improving Diabetic
Retinopathy detection.

2.1 Related Research
2.1.1 Is it Time to Replace CNNs with Transformers for Medical Images?
2.1.1.1 Summary
The paper[ 11] investigates whether Vision Transformers (ViTs) can effectively replace Con-
volutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for medical image diagnosis. The authors explore the
performance of ViTs versus CNNs across three medical imaging datasets, including APTOS
2019, a dataset for Diabetic Retinopathy detection. They compare the models under three ini-
tialization strategies: random initialization, ImageNet pretraining, and self-supervised learning.
The results show that CNNs outperform ViTs when trained from scratch, but when pretrained
on ImageNet, ViTs match CNNs in performance. Additionally, ViTs show a small advantage
when combined with self-supervised pretraining.
2.1.1.2 Critical Analysis
The paper provides a valuable comparison between CNNs and ViTs for medical imaging tasks,
which is a timely and important investigation given the rising popularity of ViTs. A strength
of the research is the inclusion of self-supervised learning and transfer learning strategies, both
of which are highly relevant to domains like medical imaging where labeled data is limited.
However, the study reveals that ViTs generally require large amounts of data or pretraining to
perform on par with CNNs, which could be a limitation for smaller datasets. Moreover, while
ViTs provide better explainability through attention maps, the improvements in performance
over CNNs are marginal in most cases with low-data regime.

2.1.2 Conv-ViT: A Convolution and Vision Transformer-Based Hybrid
Feature Extraction Method for Retinal Disease Detection
2.1.2.1 Summary
The paper[22] presents a hybrid model combining CNNs and ViT to detect retinal diseases. The
model integrates pre-trained CNN models (Inception-V3 and ResNet-50) to extract texture-
based features and a ViT to capture shape-based features from optical coherence tomography
(OCT) images. The fusion of CNN and ViT features enhances the ability to classify retinal
diseases into four categories: Choroidal Neovascularization (CNV), Diabetic Macular Edema
(DME), DRUSEN, and NORMAL. The model achieves a weighted average classification ac-
curacy of 94%, indicating the effectiveness of combining both local texture and global shape
features.
2.1.2.2 Critical analysis
The Conv-ViT model presents a novel approach by fusing CNN-based texture feature extraction
with transformer-based shape feature learning. A key strength of the study is the use of both
Inception-V3 and ResNet-50 CNN models to address texture features while adding the ViT to
capture long-range pixel relationships. This hybrid approach improves the robustness of retinal
disease classification, outperforming single-model approaches. However, the complexity of
the model (with three deep learning models) may limit its practicality for real-time applications
due to computational demands. Additionally, the dataset used is imbalanced, which may have
affected the performance on minority classes like DRUSEN, which had a lower F1 score.

2.1.3 Scopeformer: n-CNN-ViT Hybrid Model for Intracranial
2.1.3.1 Summary
The research[33] paper proposes a hybrid architecture combining multiple CNNs with a ViT
for classifying intracranial hemorrhages. The authors utilize a feature generation backbone
comprising several CNNs, including Xception networks, to create feature maps that enhance
the ViT’s attention mechanism. The CNNs are pretrained using different paradigms such as
ImageNet and GAN-based datasets, enabling the ViT to attend to rich and multi-level feature
representations. This hybrid model achieves a classification accuracy of 98.04%, demonstrating
its effectiveness and scalability for medical image analysis.
2.1.3.2 Critical analysis
The research highlights a novel combination of CNN and ViT models, showcasing the strength
of using CNNs as feature extractors for the ViT. One of the strengths is the use of multiple
pretrained CNNs, which improves the diversity and richness of the feature maps. However,
while the hybrid model is scalable, the computational complexity of stacking multiple CNNs
could be a drawback for systems with limited resources. Nonetheless, the paper effectively
demonstrates the benefit of integrating CNNs into ViT-based architectures for complex medical
tasks.
2.1.3.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The approach of using CNNs as feature extractors to improve the ViT’s attention mechanism is
directly applicable. Both tasks involve medical image classification, where the combination of
local feature extraction (via CNNs) and global attention (via ViT) can enhance model perfor-
mance. The findings from this paper, such as the modular scalability and the impact of using
multiple pretrained CNNs, can inform the design and optimization of the proposed Diabetic
Retinopathy detection model.

2.1.4 Collaborative Consultation Doctors Model: Unifying CNN and ViT
for COVID-19 Diagnostic
2.1.4.1 Summary
The paper[44] proposes a hybrid model combining CNNs and ViTs for diagnosing COVID-19
using chest X-ray and CT scan images. The model draws inspiration from the collaborative
consultation process in medical diagnostics, where multiple experts consult to achieve more
accurate diagnoses. The CNN extracts local spatial features, while the ViT captures global
contextual information. The authors employ an early fusion strategy, where CNN and ViT
features are combined and then passed through a residual neural network. The model is eval-
uated on two benchmark datasets—ChestXray and Clean-CC-CCII—achieving accuracy rates
of 98.86% and 95.62%, respectively.
2.1.4.2 Critical analysis
This paper presents an innovative approach by merging the strengths of CNNs and ViTs in
medical diagnostics, emphasizing the collaboration between local feature extraction and global
attention mechanisms. One of the strengths of the research is the use of early feature fusion,
which maximizes the complementary capabilities of both models, leading to superior accuracy
on COVID-19 diagnosis. Additionally, the analogy to a collaborative consultation between
doctors provides a compelling conceptual framework for combining different models. How-
ever, the computational complexity of using both CNN and ViT models in parallel might be a
barrier for real-time or resource-constrained deployments in clinical settings.
2.1.4.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
Both models aim to combine CNNs for local feature extraction and ViTs for global context
analysis. The early fusion strategy discussed in the paper offers valuable insights into how we
can structure the fusion of CNN and ViT features in our model. Additionally, the paper’s strong
results on medical image classification tasks suggest that a similar approach could improve the
performance of our hybrid model for Diabetic Retinopathy detection, especially when dealing
with the complexities of retinal image classification.

2.1.5 Combining the Transformer and Convolution for Effective Brain
Tumor Classification Using MRI Images
2.1.5.1 Summary
The research[55] explores a hybrid model combining CNNs and ViTs for brain tumor classifi-
cation using MRI images. The authors propose a Transformer-Enhanced Convolutional Neural
Network (TECNN) model, where the CNN is responsible for extracting local features, while the
ViT captures global dependencies using self-attention. The paper demonstrates the effective-
ness of this hybrid model on two datasets: BraTS 2018 and Figshare, achieving accuracy rates
of 96.75% and 99.10%, respectively. The authors introduce Feature Fusion Module (FFM) and
Intelligent Merge Module (IMM) to combine CNN feature maps with Transformer patches,
which ensures that both local and global information are retained and used for classification.
2.1.5.2 Critical analysis
This research presents a strong case for hybrid architectures that leverage both CNNs and ViTs
for medical image classification tasks. One of its strengths is the introduction of FFM and IMM,
which addresses the semantic gap between CNN feature maps and Transformer embeddings,
resulting in more effective feature fusion. The TECNN model outperforms state-of-the-art
methods in the datasets it was tested on, demonstrating its robustness. However, while the
model achieves high accuracy, it relies on a complex architecture that may be computationally
expensive, making it challenging to implement in resource-constrained environments.
2.1.5.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The TECNN model’s approach of using CNNs for local feature extraction and ViTs for global
feature extraction involves medical image classification, and the hybrid architecture’s ability
to combine spatial and global information can be applied to our Diabetic Retinopathy dataset.
The FFM and IMM modules in TECNN offer a valuable reference for effectively merging
CNN and ViT outputs, which can be integrated into our model to ensure that both local (retinal
features) and global (contextual) information are preserved for accurate diagnosis. This paper’s
methodology and findings can provide insights into optimizing our hybrid model for enhanced
performance.

3.2 Methodology
Our methodology for Diabetic Retinopathy detection involves a hybrid deep learning model
that integrates the strengths of CNNs and ViTs. This method leverages CNNs for local feature
extraction and ViTs for capturing global dependencies in retinal fundus images, enhancing
classification accuracy for Diabetic Retinopathy stages.
3.2.1 Input Image Data
We begin by using retinal fundus images as input to the model. The images are resized to
a standard resolution of 224×224×3 to maintain uniformity across the dataset and facilitate
efficient processing by both the CNN and ViT components.
3.2.2 Preprocessing and Data Augmentation
Prior to analysis, the images undergo preprocessing to improve the quality and robustness of
the model:
• Normalization: Pixel values are normalized to reduce intensity variations across images.
• Data Augmentation: Techniques such as random rotation, flipping, and zooming are
applied to enhance the model’s ability to generalize and reduce overfitting.
3.2.3 CNN Feature Extraction
To extract local features from the retinal images, we employ multiple pre-trained CNN models
(e.g., ResNet-50, DenseNet-201) that capture fine-grained details such as blood vessel patterns,
3.2 Methodology
exudates and microaneurysms. The feature maps generated by each CNN are combined and
aligned to provide a comprehensive representation of the retinal images.
3.2.4 ViT Attention Encoding
After the CNN feature extraction step, the resulting feature maps are passed into a Vision
Transformer (ViT) model. The ViT processes these feature maps through self-attention layers
to capture long-range dependencies and global contextual information, ensuring that the model
can analyze the entire image holistically.
3.2.5 Classification and Prediction
Finally, the output from the ViT is passed through a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) for classi-
fication. The MLP is responsible for predicting the presence and severity of Diabetic Retinopa-
thy based on the combined local and global features extracted by the CNN and ViT com-
ponents. The model outputs the predicted class labels corresponding to different stages of
Diabetic Retinopathy.
This hybrid CNN-ViT model is designed to leverage the strengths of both architectures to
achieve more accurate and robust Diabetic Retinopathy detection and classification.

Conclusions and Future Work
This project proposes a hybrid model that combines Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
and Vision Transformers (ViTs) to enhance the detection and classification of Diabetic Retinopa-
thy. The proposed approach aims to leverage CNNs for local feature extraction and ViTs
for capturing global dependencies, addressing the limitations of each architecture individu-
ally. While the model has not been fully developed, the design demonstrates the potential
for improving diagnostic accuracy in early detection of Diabetic Retinopathy. By integrating
both deep learning techniques, this approach could provide a more efficient and accurate tool
for healthcare professionals in the future. Further research and development are required to
validate this approach through real-world testing and optimization for practical deployment.
