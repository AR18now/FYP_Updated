1. Introduction
1.2 Research Problem Statement
Current AI-based driver monitoring systems, such as those developed by Motive and other
industry leaders, focus on detecting high-risk behaviors such as yawning, eyes closed, eat-
ing, drinking, mobile phone usage, seat belt violation, etc. These behaviors are often one
of the leading reasons why fatal crashes happen in truck driving. While AI-based driver
monitoring systems have evolved considerably in the past few years, their performance is
still majorly defined by the data upon which they are trained.
These models are trained using large amounts of annotated real-life data to effectively
learn to predict high-risk behaviors. However, some of these behaviours such as fatigue-
induced yawning or not wearing a seatbelt, are often underrepresented in various datasets.
For example, suppose there is a 24-hour video of a person driving, around less than 1
percent of that timeframe would include a person yawning. So when there is a lesser
quantity of this type of data, the features predicting this behavior would have a lower
precision and recall. This lack of diversity and well-distribution of the dataset will lead
to suboptimal performance. Consequently, the system may not detect a fatigued driver,
potentially resulting in life-threatening accidents.
Moreover, the process of collecting and annotating driver behavior is very costly and
time-consuming. Without a more efficient way for this procedure, the effectiveness of
this system will remain limited.
1.3 Software Requirements Specification
This section outlines the functional and non-functional requirements for AutoMorph,
which improves driver monitoring systems by generating synthetic images through dif-
fusion models. The software will support image augmentation techniques such as in-
painting, pose augmentation, and text-conditioned generation to create realistic synthetic
data.
The successful implementation of AutoMorph depends on meeting the following criteria:
1.3.1 Functional Requirements
1. Driver Image Augmentation:
• The system shall augment existing driver-facing images with underrepresented
risky behaviors such as yawning, and eyes-closing.
• Inpainting techniques will be used to target specific regions of the image for
augmentation (e.g., mouth for yawning, eyes for closure).

2. Pose Augmentation:
• The system shall modify the posture of the driver using a DragDiffusion-like
functionality. (e.g., shifting the position of a phone or turning the head).
3. Text-Based Conditioning:
• The system shall support text-based prompts to guide the image generation
process, enabling controlled outputs based on the specific conditions described
in the text (e.g., “Generate a person yawning with eyes closed”).
4. Dataset Integration:
• The augmented images shall be seamlessly integrated into existing datasets to
improve the accuracy of AI-based driver monitoring systems.
1.3.2 Non-Functional Requirements
1. Performance:
• The synthetic image generation process should operate efficiently, augmenting
at least 20-30% of the existing dataset within a reasonable timeframe, without
compromising quality.
• Generated images must closely mimic the real-world counterparts, ensuring
that the synthetic data does not appear artificial to AI systems during training.
2. Scalability:
• The software should support scalable generation, handling an increasing num-
ber of images or new classes of driver behaviors without significant degrada-
tion in performance.
3. Usability:
• The system shall provide an interface for easy integration of augmented im-
ages into the existing driver monitoring AI pipeline for retraining and perfor-
mance evaluation.

2.1 Related Research
2.1.1 High-Resolution Image Synthesis with Latent Diffusion Models
(2022)
2.1.1.1 Summary of the research item
This article presents Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) for the task of high-resolution im-
age synthesis. LDMs extend the diffusion process over a latent space. Thereafter, this
latent is rendered into a high-res image. This approach cuts computation cost by a large
degree compared to the pixel-space diffusion models and at the same level or even better
image quality is obtained.
2.1.1.2 Critical analysis of the research item
They argue that the efficient and scalable approach of this paper for high-resolution image
synthesis is quite novel. However, that comes with difficulties as the latent space repre-
sentations employed are rather critical and may be problematic with large dimensional
datasets or detailed images. The extent to which this will affect practical applications
remains to be established.

2.1 Related Research
2.1.1.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
This paper will form the basis for understanding latent diffusion models and in particular
their application in high-resolution image synthesis.
2.1.2 Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models (DDIM) (2021)
2.1.2.1 Summary of the research item
DDIM uses a new framework for denoising diffusion models that have improved sampling
efficiency. They utilize implicit models that allow faster generation of samples whilst
maintaining relatively high-quality outputs. Their approach in particular has reduced the
number of steps required for the diffusion process which in turn, accelerates the image
synthesis process.
2.1.2.2 Critical analysis of the research item
DDIM provides major improvements with respect to sampling speed. Their method may
introduce fidelity issues which is a fair trade-off considering the speed boost they have
achieved.
2.1.2.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
DDIM will be looked at and used in the image generation process to speed up the image
synthesis process.

Point-based Image Editing (2023)
2.1.3.1 Summary of the research item
DragDiffusion is a successor paper to DragGAN where an image’s context is morphed

DragDiffusion allows a modest improvement in image modification however, this method

DragDiffusion’s method will be recreated and used for pose augmentation during Iteration

2.1.4 SDXL: Scaling Diffusion Models for High-Resolution Image
Synthesis (2023)
2.1.4.1 Summary of the research item
Stable Diffusion XL focussed on scaling their predecessor model (Stable diffusion) for an
even better performance on high quality image synthesis. The paper delved into various
techniques for optimizing model architechture as well as introducing it’s own V AE and a
two-stage process with the second stage refining the image.
2.1.4.2 Critical analysis of the research item
Albeit this model has significantly improved diffusion model, the scalability of this model
is very limited due to the sheer amounts of VRAM the model requires.
2.1.4.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
If there are no hardware blockers on utlizing this architecture then we will upgrade our
backbone latent diffusion model to this one. In worst-case scenario, the conditioning
techniques can be used from this paper.

3.1 Dataset
For this project, we will be utilizing the 100-Driver dataset, which consists of 470,000
images taken from 4 cameras that observe 100 drivers over 79 hours of driving from 5
different vehicles. This dataset offers substantial variability, which makes it ideal for
building robust driver safety systems. The 100-Driver dataset covers key variations that
are crucial for real-world applications, including:
• Changes in vehicle types
• Variations in driver appearances and behaviors
• Different camera viewpoints
• Diverse lighting conditions (day/night, shadows, etc.)
The presence of these variations makes the dataset highly representative of real-world
scenarios, thus ensuring that our synthetic data augmentations are both effective and prac-
tical.

3.2 Bounding Box Extraction with Owl-ViT
To ensure that we focus on key regions of interest, such as the driver’s face, hands, and
objects in the environment, we will employ Owl-ViT v2. Owl-ViT (Object-aware Vision
Transformer) allows us to perform image-caption-based detection. By providing an im-
age along with a prompt describing the object or region of interest, Owl-ViT will output
bounding boxes with associated confidence scores.
3.2.1 Process
The process for extracting bounding boxes from images in the 100-Driver dataset using
Owl-ViT consists of the following steps:
1. Input an image from the dataset into Owl-ViT v2.
2. Provide a prompt mentioning the object or area of interest (e.g., "driver’s face",
"steering wheel", "mobile phone").
3. Owl-ViT processes the image and outputs a bounding box around the specified area
along with a confidence score.

3.3 Dreambooth Inpainting for Yawning Model
In this section, we propose a method to train a yawning model by leveraging a Dreambooth-
like inpainting approach. The goal is to generate synthetic images of drivers yawning by
manipulating existing images through a combination of latent space encoding, masking,
and noise prediction. Below is the detailed process for training and inference using this
approach.
3.3.1 Training Process
The training process involves several key steps, starting with data preparation, followed by
latent encoding, noise prediction, and backpropagation to refine the model. The following
steps outline the full pipeline:
3.3.1.1 Data Preparation and Bounding Box Selection
Once we have extracted the bounding boxes for the driver’s face or relevant regions (as
discussed in the previous section), we proceed with centering the bounding box and cre-
ating a mask over the detected region.
Figure 3.4: Center-cropped image based on bounding box for inpainting.
Figure 3.5: Masked image showing the region of interest for yawning inpainting.

3. Proposed Approach
3.3.1.2 Latent Encoding using VQ-V AE and Mask Creation
We utilize the VQ-V AE (Vector Quantized Variational Autoencoder) fromCompVis/latent-
diffusion to encode the input image into latent representations. A similar process is ap-
plied to the mask, where the bounding box is used to create the masked latent image.
• Image Encoding: The image is passed through the VQ-V AE model, which encodes
it into a latent space representation.
• Masked Latent Encoding: The masked portion of the image is also encoded using
VQ-V AE to create a masked latent representation.
Figure 3.6: Latent encoding of the original and masked image.
3.3.1.3 Noising and Concatenation for Training
Once the latent image and masked latent image are generated, we apply noise to the latent
image. The noisy latent image is then concatenated with the mask and masked latent
image, creating a 7-channel input that will be passed through the U-Net.
• Noising the Latent Image: Gaussian noise is added to the latent image.
• Concatenation: The noisy latent image, mask, and masked latent image are con-
catenated to form the input tensor.
Figure 3.7: Concatenation of noisy latent, mask, and masked latent image.

3.3 Dreambooth Inpainting for Yawning Model
3.3.1.4 Training the U-Net
The U-Net model is responsible for predicting the noise added to the latent image. The
training objective is to minimize the difference between the actual noise added and the
noise predicted by the U-Net. This is achieved through backpropagation, allowing the
U-Net to improve its noise prediction capability over time.
• Noise Prediction: The U-Net predicts the noise added to the image.
• Loss Function: The difference between the actual and predicted noise is minimized
using a standard loss function (e.g., MSE).
• Backpropagation: The model weights are updated based on the error signal from
the loss function.
Figure 3.8: U-Net training to predict noise added to the latent image.
3.3.2 Inference Process
During inference, the trained model will be used to generate yawning images by denoising
a random latent noise vector iteratively. The following steps outline the inference pipeline:
3.3.2.1 Latent Noise and Masked Image Input
For inference, we start by providing:
• Random latent noise
• A mask of the region where yawning will be synthesized (e.g., mouth region)
• A masked image of a normal person (e.g., with a closed mouth or normal expres-
sion)
These three inputs are concatenated to create the input for the U-Net.
15
3. Proposed Approach
3.3.2.2 Iterative Denoising Process
The U-Net is used iteratively to denoise the latent representation over a fixed number of
steps, gradually turning the random noise into a realistic image of a driver who is yawning.
• Denoising: The U-Net progressively denoises the latent representation, refining the
details in each step.
• Fixed Steps: The process is repeated for a pre-defined number of steps until a fully
realistic image is generated.
Figure 3.9: Iterative denoising process to generate yawning images.
3.3.2.3 Latent Decoding to Obtain Final Image
Once the latent representation has been fully denoised, it is decoded back into an image
using the VQ-V AE decoder. The final image should depict the driver with a yawn.
Figure 3.10: Final yawning image decoded from the denoised latent representation.
This Dreambooth-style approach of inpainting with diffusion models allows us to generate
synthetic images of drivers displaying underrepresented behaviors like yawning, which
can then be used to augment the existing dataset and improve the performance of driver
safety systems.
