2.1 Related Research Papers
2.1.1 Application and Difference of Raster and Vector Graphics [ 33]
This research paper focuses on the characteristics, applications, and distinctions between
raster and vector graphics, offering insights into how these two types of computer graph-
ics/fonts are used across various domains.
2.1.1.1 Summary
The paper presents an in-depth overview of raster and vector graphics, emphasizing their
fundamental differences. Raster graphics are described as pixel-based images, where
each pixel has its own defined color, forming the image in a grid-like structure. Vector
graphics, on the other hand, utilize geometric shapes and mathematical equations to create
images. This makes them scalable without loss of quality, a significant advantage over
raster images. The study also discusses image formats, such as BMP, JPEG for raster and
EPS, AI, and SVG for vector graphics. It elaborates on the software tools commonly used
for each type, with Adobe Photoshop and Corel Photo-Paint being key for raster images,
while Adobe Illustrator, Corel Draw, and CAD programs are integral to vector graphics.

2.1.1.2 Critical Analysis
The strength of this paper lies in its clear differentiation between raster and vector graph-
ics, which is essential for users needing to decide which type of image to use in specific
applications. The comparison of image formats and software tools adds practical value.
However, the paper tends to focus more on the technical aspects without deeply explor-
ing the evolving trends in hybrid graphics technologies or how advances in compression
algorithms and hardware have started to blur the boundaries between raster and vector
graphics. Furthermore, while the paper offers detailed descriptions of graphic types, it
lacks practical data or case studies that could provide more concrete examples of their
applications in modern industries.
2.1.1.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The topic of raster and vector graphics in this study is directly related to the proposed re-
search on feature extraction in raster font images using TTF files. The exploration of how
raster images handle pixel information and the comparison of resolution scalability offers
valuable background information for understanding the challenges in feature extraction
of raster fonts from their images. Additionally, the software tools mentioned in the pa-
per, such as Adobe Photoshop and Corel Photo-Paint, align with the research focusing on
the processing of raster images. Information about the vector images and the technical
comparisons will help in understanding image processing within the study.
2.1.2 TrueType Transformer: Character and Font Style Recognition
in Outline Format [44]
This paper reviews the TrueType Transformer (T3), a model designed to classify fonts
and recognize character and style based on outline formats.
2.1.2.1 Summary
The study proposes a novel approach to font recognition that uses outline-based data
rather than pixel-based images/data. The model is intended to process control points
that determine the outlines of characters, allowing recognition of both character and font
styles. By visualizing attention weights in the Transformer model, the study determines
which control points are most significant for classification, providing greater insights into
font/typeface structure. The study also compares T3’s performance to traditional ap-
proaches such as ResNet, finding comparable accuracy for character identification and an
advantage in style recognition due to precise structural information from control points. It

2.1.2.2 Critical Analysis
The TrueType Transformer model has the strengths of Transformer architectures in han-

tain clarity across various dimensions. Additionally, exploring the handling of off-curve
points in the T3 model may provide an opportunity to improve recognition tasks based on
subtle structural cues.

This paper addresses the challenge of font identification in document images and empha-
sizes the complexities of visual font recognition, and proposes data augmentation tech-
niques such as scaling and rotation to enhance model robustness.
2.1.3.1 Summary
The research presents a systematic approach to font identification through the use of a
CNN model trained on a comprehensive dataset of 10,000 fonts. The authors first prepro-
cess document images to extract individual characters and then apply a max-voting policy
to improve recognition accuracy. The experiments conducted on synthetic and natural

One of the strengths of this study is its comprehensive dataset, which allows for an exten-

2.1.4.1 Summary
The paper presents a deep learning framework designed to identify fonts in images. The
authors developed a system that uses convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to extract
font features from images of text. The methodology includes training a deep netcwork
using a large dataset of labeled fonts, followed by performance evaluation on various test

font recognition tasks, outperforming existing techniques. The study concludes that the
DeepFont system can effectively identify fonts in images, thus providing a significant
advancement in the field of font recognition.
2.1.4.2 Critical Analysis
The strengths of this research include its innovative approach to using deep learning for
font identification, which demonstrates the potential of CNNs in processing visual data.
Adobe TypeKit dataset is used for CNN Training, which contains a diverse collection of
font styles and variations. The dataset includes more than 1,000 fonts, capturing a wide
range of typographic styles. However, the study has certain weaknesses, such as limited
exploration of real-world scenarios where images may contain noise or distortions, which
can affect accuracy. Additionally, relying on labeled datasets may introduce biases, as the
model’s performance is dependent on the quality and diversity of the training data.
2.1.4.3 Relationship to the proposed research work
The findings from the DeepFont study significantly inform the proposed research on font
classification. The use of deep learning techniques parallels the objectives of this study,
particularly in automating font classification and identifying distinct features among raster
and vector fonts. Furthermore, the gaps identified in the DeepFont paper, such as handling
noisy images and dataset bias, highlight areas for improvement in the current research. By
building upon the methodologies of DeepFont while addressing its limitations, this study
aims to enhance raster font classification systems, thus contributing to the modern font
technologies.

Chapter 3
Proposed Approach
A way forward is to find modern vector fonts (TrueType Formal/Open Type Format) that
resemble with the existing raster fonts currently in use. Our assigned project is to research
and develop a web-based solution that facilitate the client and automatically search for
the latest font to which the old undesired Raster fonts are similar. The process will be to
compare them to a library of TTF/OTF fonts, and find the best match in terms of their
font properties like shape, size and style.
The methodology that we have determined till now from relevant research papers includes
the following steps:
3.1 Font Feature Extraction and Preprocessing
The first step of the approach involves extracting key visual features from both raster and
vector fonts. The challenge lies in analyzing raster font images, which are pixel-based,
and comparing them with vector fonts like TrueType Fonts (TTF) and OpenType Fonts
(OTF), which are defined by mathematical outlines. The process includes:
• Data Generation:
The generation of font data is an essential step in building a diverse dataset for
model training and testing. In this project, existing raster fonts are generated pro-
grammatically using Python scripts, which render characters from raster font files
(e.g., TTF or OTF formats) into images containing text in raster font. The process
involves:
– Raster Font Generation from Font Files: A Python script utilizing libraries
such as Pillow is used to create raster format images at different resolutions.
The script loads raster font file (TTF/OTF), and each character is rendered as

3. Proposed Approach
a raster image by specifying the desired pixel size and resolution. By generat-
ing raster fonts at varying sizes, the system captures the visual distortion and
pixelation that occurs during scaling, which is critical for understanding raster
font limitations.
– Multiple Resolutions and Styles: To enhance the dataset diversity, the fonts
are rendered at multiple resolutions (e.g., low to high pixel densities) and in
various styles such as bold, italic, or underlined. These variations allow the
system to learn how different styling and scaling affect raster font clarity and
sharpness.
– Data Augmentation: Further augmentations such as rotation, translation, and
noise addition are applied to the raster images to simulate real-world distor-
tions. These transformations ensure that the model can generalize well and is
robust to minor variations in the input images.
– Labeling: Each generated raster font image is labeled with metadata such as
the font family, size, style (e.g., bold, italic), and resolution. This labeling is
critical for later stages of classification, where the model will need to associate
visual features with specific font characteristics.
The vector font dataset will be taken from Google Fonts online.
• Image Segmentation: For raster fonts, the character images need to be prepro-
cessed to isolate individual letters and remove noise. This may involve converting
the images to grayscale, binarization, and edge detection to extract the character
outlines. It’s important to note that the specific preprocessing steps may vary de-
pending on the characteristics of the input images.
• Vector Data Parsing: For vector fonts, the font files (in .ttf or .otf formats) will
be parsed to retrieve their control points and outlines, which define the curves and
shapes of the characters.
• Feature Selection: Features such as aspect ratio, stroke width, curve smoothness,
and corner sharpness are extracted from both raster and vector fonts. These features
provide a basis for comparison and classification, as they define the visual style of
each font and are critical for the classification process.
3.2 Classification of Fonts
After extracting relevant features, the next step is to classify the fonts based on their style,
shape, and size. A supervised machine learning algorithm will be employed to learn the
characteristics of different fonts and classify them accordingly.
14
3.3 Font Prediction Model
3.2.1 Model Choice
The classification model is crucial for recognizing patterns in both raster and vector font
features. We will consider using a Support V ector Machine (SVM) or a Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN), noting that SVM may be preferable for simpler, linearly separable
data, while CNNs excel in handling high-dimensional, image-based data.
3.2.2 Training Process
The classifier will be trained on font datasets, where each font is labeled with its type
(raster or vector), size, style, and shape. The training process involves:
• Data Augmentation: For raster fonts, variations in scale and rotation can be ap-
plied to improve the robustness of the model.
• Cross-Validation: K-fold cross-validation will be used to ensure the model gener-
alizes well across different font styles and sizes.
• Accuracy Metrics: The model’s performance will be evaluated based on classifica-
tion accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 score to ensure accurate font classification.
3.3 Font Prediction Model
Once the fonts have been classified, the next task is to predict an equivalent vector font
for a given raster font. The prediction model will take the extracted features of a raster
font as input and output the most similar vector font in terms of size, shape, and style.
This process will involve:
3.3.1 Similarity Metric
A similarity function will be defined to compare the feature vectors of the raster font
and the vector fonts. The Euclidean distance between the feature vectors will serve as a
measure of how closely the raster font resembles a particular vector font. It is important
to specify that "size" will be assessed based on dimensions and pixel density for accurate
comparison.

3.3.2 Font Matching Algorithm
Based on the similarity metric, a heuristic-based font matching algorithm will be devel-
oped to recommend a vector font that matches the raster font. The system will map raster
font features to their corresponding vector features and predict a vector font that maintains
the closest visual consistency.
3.4 Website Development
To complement the font classification and prediction model, a simple yet effective web
application will be developed. This tool will allow users to upload raster font images and
receive high-quality, closely matched vector font results. The application aims to make
the font conversion process easy and accessible, saving users significant time and effort,
especially in scenarios where maintaining visual consistency and clarity across different
resolutions is crucial.
3.4.1 User Interface and Workflow
The web application will have a user-friendly interface, designed for both technical and
non-technical users. The workflow will be as follows:
• Font Image Upload: Users can upload images containing raster fonts. The appli-
cation will support multiple image formats such as PNG and JPEG. Each image
will undergo preprocessing to isolate individual characters and remove noise if nec-
essary.
• Font Matching and Classification: Once the raster font image is uploaded, the
backend system will process the image using the trained classification model to
identify the general type of font, while the prediction model performs the actual
matching. The system will extract relevant features from the raster font image and
compare them with the features of vector fonts in the database.
• Prediction Results: The system will output the predicted vector font (or fonts) that
best matches the uploaded raster font in terms of features extracted from the input
image such as style, size, and shape. The user will receive a new page for the vector
font display results.
• Additional Font Information: Along with the predicted font, the web applica-
tion will also provide additional information about the matched font, including its
family, style (e.g., serif, sans-serif), and characteristics (e.g., weight, stroke width).

3.4.2 Backend System and Technology
The backend system will handle all the processing, including image analysis, feature ex-
traction, font classification, and outline prediction. The system will be built using modern
web development frameworks such as Flask (for Python integration) and will be con-
nected to a machine learning model deployed on a server. The font classification and
prediction model will be trained offline and integrated into the web application for real-
time font matching.
3.4.3 Advantages and Benefits
This web application offers several advantages:
• Time-Saving: Users can quickly upload raster font images and get precise vector
font suggestions without manual searching or testing.
• Accessibility: With the simple interface, the application is accessible to designers,
developers, and non-technical users alike, making font comparison straightforward.
• Consistency and Clarity: The application ensures that raster fonts are compared
with scalable vector fonts that match the original font image’s clarity, improving
visual quality across different screen resolutions.
• Scalability: The backend system is designed to handle a high volume of font image
uploads and predictions, making it scalable for multiple users at once.
